Finally, the third chapter analyzes students' ranking and their effects on different indicators that measure academic performance in a large public university in Colombia. Like the first chapter, the analysis is centered on the labor supply and introduces some elements from psychology. The chosen university is of particular importance, as it allows low-income students to have access to quality education at negligible costs. It also faces inefficiencies, as it has high rates of expulsion and students remain enrolled for longer than expected without receiving a diploma, which affects their transition to the labor market. The next paragraphs provide a detailed summary of each chapter.
The first chapter analyzes the unintended effects of a randomized control trial in Bangladesh that targets the ultra-poor on the expectations that parents have about their children's occupations. Because significant aspirational gaps exist between poor and wealthy households, as well as between boys and girls, this chapter analyzes whether a graduation-type program can reduce these gaps. As the intervention is a composite of different services including the transfer of a productive asset, training, coaching, and a monthly stipend, among others, this research explores some of the potential channels that could explain changes in expectations, namely substitution and wealth effects, and changes in the perception of education. Further, it analyzes whether higher expectations are linked to better educational outcomes. The results show that the intervention only seems to reduce the aspirational gaps for boys, whereas for girls the intervention could be reinforcing traditional gender roles. The results also point to the importance of the coaching component in these types of interventions.
The second chapter explores causal effects of the soybean expansion in Brazil on formal jobs. Recent literature shows ambiguous socioeconomic effects of the expansion of soybean in Brazil. However, these studies are descriptive in general and fail to establish causality. Therefore, the estimated effects cannot be attributed only to the expansion. The adoption of soybeans is an endogenous process, which hinders the estimation of causal effects. This chapter estimates the causal effects of this expansion on formal employment at the municipal level. The novel empirical strategy relies on the use of Synthetic Control Matching and the definition of a control group composed by municipalities that do not produce soybeans but meet the geographical conditions to do so. Previous studies use as control group municipalities that are not well suited for agriculture. This analysis focuses only on the municipalities that comprise the Brazilian agricultural frontier.
The results show that the expansion led to increases in formal jobs in agriculture but did not have any effects on other sectors. There are also significant heterogeneities among the municipalities regarding the magnitude of the effects. Using tools from the regional economics literature, the estimations also point to possible economies of specialization in the region.
The third chapter analyzes ranking effects in a large public university in Colombia on various college outcomes measured at different times. In particular, it focuses on three questions: first, whether students who were at the top of their classes in high school react in the same way as average (or bottom-ranked) students do when both experience low performance in the first semester of college; second, whether studying with higher performing peers translates into better academic outcomes, as shown by the tradeoff between studying with high performing peers and ranking effects; and third, whether students who receive an academic probation in the first semester of college perform differently than students with similar grades. The results show that changes in rankings do not seem to have any negative effect for students, as higher high school rankings are usually correlated with better educational outcomes, independent of students' performance in the first semester. This finding is in line with previous research about the long-lasting effects of students' rankings. Likewise, students enrolled with higher performing peers experience significant decreases in their performance. Receiving an academic probation does not translate into significantly better academic performance. The calculation of rankings and competitiveness of cohorts could provide useful tools for colleges to analyze which students might be at risk of low academic performance.
Parental expectations can exert a strong influence over children's decisions. For example, Ahiapor and Swaray (2015) find that male parents in rural Ghana are more likely to invest in boys' education than girls' education, as they expect higher returns from this investment. Favara (2007) finds that children's aspirations about education in Ethiopia mirror their parents'; thus, low aspirations from either children or parents can lead to lower investments in education. In poor rural households in Bangladesh, often parents make all the decisions regarding children's schooling and marriage (Arafat, Ahmad, & Ismail, 2021) . Policies that target parents, as in the case of asset transfers or training, but do not target children directly could have significant effects on children's education and their future occupations. This research analyzes some of the unintended effects of a policy intervention targeting women in Bangladesh on expectations about children and the potential mechanisms though which this policy affects expectations.
Recent literature has found that high educational and occupational aspirations usually lead to higher investments in education and educational attainment, and that this pattern follows an inverse u-shaped behavior. In this way, when aspirations are set relatively high and become unrealistic, investments in the future (education, assets) decrease, as individuals perceive they are unlikely to reach such goals (Ray, 2006; Janzen et al., 2017) . Although in this research we refer to expectations and aspirations interchangeably, we focus on the former. The main difference between the two concepts is that aspirations have no constraints, as individuals select what they desire independent of their current conditions (income, education, etc.), whereas expectations are bounded by their current situations (Janzen et al., 2017) .
Aspirations (and expectations) are influenced by the socioeconomic context and cultural norms. In an influential paper, Appadurai (2004) argues that poor households have lower capacity to "aspire" because of their lack of opportunities. This means that the poor lack the experiences, information, and the material means to explore different options for their future. In this vein, Dalton, Ghosal, and Mani (2014) argue that this "aspiration failure" is a consequence of poverty, and this causality does not apply in the opposite direction. Subsequently, it is not surprising that parents from the poorest backgrounds are less likely to expect their children to go to college or have access to high-paid jobs (Favara, 2007) . Although income plays a major role in shaping expectations, aspects such as gender or living in rural areas are also important determinants of expectations.
Bangladesh has characteristics of a "patriarchal" society, where men have more opportunities outside the household and control over assets than women do. For example, practices such as transferring assets and properties through the male line, which could leave women under resourced and more vulnerable to poverty, are common in the country (Kabeer, Huq, & Rahaman, 2021) . Similarly, the Purdah norms reduce women's mobility, restricting their economic activities to those that could be performed within the household (Kabeer, Mahmud, & Tasneem, 2018) .
Consequently, most women in rural Bangladesh are occupied in household chores. Although initiatives such as the Female Secondary School Stipend program (FSSSP), which covers school fees and provides a stipend for secondary education to girls in rural areas, had significant effects on improving access to jobs and decreasing early marriages (Hahn, Islam, Nuzhat, Smyth, and Yang, 2017) , the expectations about boys and girls in society are still widely different. While it is commonly expected for girls to secure a "good" marriage, as dictated by the gender norms, boys enjoy more flexibility (Arafat, Ahmad, and Ismail 2021) . Given this context, it is not surprising that some of the main causes for dropping out of school for girls in poor rural areas are related to early marriages or household chores, whereas for boys it is usually work (Hasnat, 2017) . At the same time, this context leads women to be economically dependent on their spouses (Arends-Kuenning & Amin, 2001) , and consequently, parents prioritize their daughters' marriages over their education.
Although expectations are heavily dependent on the context, different studies have shown that individuals can be influenced to modify their expectations. Expectations can be shaped through the influence of role models, such as in the case of India, where girls in regions with reserved quotas for women in leadership positions were more likely to aspire to jobs that require an education (Beaman, Duflo, Pande & Topalova, 2012) . They could also be influenced through inspirational speeches, as in the case of Ethiopia, where parents who were exposed to motivational speeches from local leaders as part of a randomized control trial changed their expectations about their children's education and increased their expenditures on education, which translated into higher enrollment and hours devoted to studying for children (Bernard, Dercon, Orkin & Taffesse, 2019) . Interactions with social leaders can also affect expectations. For example, women participating in a conditional cash transfer program (CCT) in Nicaragua and who were exposed to frequent interactions with social leaders invested more in education and diversified their income sources (Macours and Vakis, 2009) . Thus, interventions that produce positive changes in expectations can lead to significant improvements in education and reductions in child marriage.
This research analyzes how expectations about children's occupations are affected by the graduation program from the Bangladesh Rural Advancement Committee (BRAC) called Targeting the Ultra-poor (TUP), where participants receive a one-time transfer to invest in productive assets (predominantly livestock), as well as training, coaching, and a stipend before the asset starts producing. It also explores some of the potential channels thorough which the intervention produces effects. First is through a wealth effect, as households experience reduced stress levels as their constraints caused by the lack of income are eased. This easing of constraints makes it easier for households to consider long term investments, such as education. The second channel is through a substitution effect, as owning the productive asset (usually livestock) can increase the opportunity costs of children's time spent studying and create tradeoffs between the time devoted to studying and the time children spend work with the asset. The third channel involves changes in parents' perceptions about education, as it is included in the coaching/life skills training component of the program. Further, the paper explores whether the effects on expectations are linked to changes in educational outcomes, such as changes in enrollment and drop-out rates.
Because "low expectations" may lead to lower educational achievement, it is important to understand whether and how policy interventions such as graduation programs affect participants'
expectations. Previous research has found that the BRAC TUP program led to significant increases in household welfare, particularly in food security, consumption, asset accumulation, income, and savings (Bandiera et al. 2017) . Households who received the treatment also experienced increases in education expenditures and on the time that children spent in livestock activities (Sulaiman, 2015; Bandiera et al., 2017) . However, none of these studies report significant changes in educational attainment and enrollment. This lack of significant results could be due to the program not incentivizing (or disincentivizing) households to pursue higher educational attainments. It could also be the result of households not being able to translate higher expectations into behaviors that could lead them to fulfill these expectations (i.e., expecting children to obtain high grades but increasing the time they spend in household chores).
This research also explores whether the intervention is producing effects that challenge gender norms, which could contribute to reducing the disparities between men and women in Bangladesh in terms of access to jobs and economic independence. For example, the main occupation for mothers in our dataset is household chores (87.6%), followed by agricultural laborers (4.6%) and maids (4.5%). We do so by exploring differential effects of the BRAC-TUP intervention on expectations by gender. Further, as we explore the effects of the program on the mindset of participants (expectations), these effects could prevail in the future, even after beneficiaries experience negative income shocks or lose assets. This is particularly important, as previous research found that some beneficiaries from BRAC-TUP fell back to the initial levels of consumption and welfare after 4 years of the intervention (Bandiera et al 2017 , Balboni, Bandiera, Burgess, Ghatak, & Heil, 2022) foot_0 , or resorted to activities such as begging (Misha, Raza, & Ara, 2019) and did not "graduate" from poverty.
This research contributes to the literature about the non-economic and unintended effects of graduation programs. Previous studies have analyzed the effects on the perception of physical and mental health, using measures such as happiness, anxiety, and stress levels, political awareness, and the participation of women in decision making and control over the resources received from the program (Roy et al. 2015; Bandiera et al, 2017) . However, no other studies have analyzed the effects of the program on expectations. Additionally, though graduation programs have different components that are difficult to disentangle, this research points to the potential importance of some of these components and their effects on expectations. Literature about conditional and unconditional cash transfer programs has explored how expectations and aspirations are affected mostly through wealth effects and the conditionality of the transfer (Gebremariam, Lodigiani, & Pasini, 2017; Cirillo, 2019; Garcia, Harker, & Ricaurte, 2019) .
Although this literature explores contexts different from Bangladesh, such as Latin America and Africa, some papers like Cirillo (2019) suggests that mechanisms such as having a sense of belonging could be more important than merely the wealth effects. Nevertheless, the effects on expectations of more complex interventions than CCTs, such as graduation programs, have not received much attention.
Our research also explores heterogenous effects based on differences in expectations. In general, the evaluations of the graduation programs focus on the average effects (Kabeer, 2019) , and the heterogeneity is usually based solely on income percentiles (as in Bandiera et al., 2017) ;
we argue that heterogenous expectations could lead to different outcomes. In this way, we analyze whether expectations are correlated with observable behaviors, such as enrollment and drop-out rates. Finally, we connect our research to the literature on poverty traps, both in the standard definition, where a poverty cutoff is measured in terms of the lack of income or wealth, as in the case of Balboni et al (2022) for Bangladesh, as well as with more recent approaches that consider both internal and external constraints (Lybbert & Wydick, 2018) . External constraints refer to income and wealth, whereas internal constraints are related to the perceptions and beliefs of individuals about their lives. In this way, interventions are successful when households can overcome both types of constraints.

Although the participants were also surveyed in 2014 and 2018, these follow-up surveys were not available for this project. The intervention, as is the case for most graduation programs, includes several components: a) the transfer of productive assets, mainly a combination of different livestock packages (including cows, goats, and poultry) valued at approximately USD$560 in PPP terms. Households were encouraged to hold the asset for at least 2 years; b) technical training with livestock specialists, focused on the best practices associated with the different stages of livestock production; c) lifeskills awareness and coaching sessions, which include topics such as hygiene, nutrition, family planning, savings, marriage, legal and political awareness, the importance of children's education, among other topics; d) weekly cash stipends for the first 40 weeks after the asset is transferred (Bandiera et al., 2017 , Gulesci, 2021) .
Although the program targets only the Ultra poor households (who represent around 6% of the country's total population), the dataset BRAC-TUP also follows households who are not eligible to participate in the program. This feature provides information to evaluate whether the gap between wealthier households and participants has been closing. The program has two sets of criteria for eligibility: exclusion and inclusion criteria. Eligible households must satisfy at least three (out of five) inclusion criteria and none of the exclusion criteria (see Krishna, Poghosyan and Das, 2012; Bandiera et al, 2017) foot_2 .
The selection process for BRAC-TUP uses a series of steps, following a randomized control trial (RCT) at the BRAC branch level. First, households are classified into four groups depending on their income level: ultra-poor, other-poor, middle-class, and upper-class. As previously mentioned, only the ultra-poor are eligible for treatment. Then, the program selects the subdistricts with two BRAC branches and randomly selects the villages located within 8 Km of these branches. The branches (and its surrounding villages) are assigned to either treatment or control groups. All eligible households located in the villages assigned to the treatment group receive the package of benefits described previously. Finally, villages assigned to the control group received the assets and technical training in 2011 (after the second follow-up), while the treatment group received the assistance in 2007. This design is proposed to prevent spillovers at the village level (Bandiera et al., 2017 , Gulesci, 2021) . The goal of the program is to "graduate" households from poverty using a combination of components. The reasoning behind providing ultra-poor households with livestock is explained as wealthier households (non-poor) rely more on livestock activities and less on seasonal labor than poor households do.
The dataset contains information about the expectations of both parents about the expected occupation of their eldest son and eldest daughter for each survey wave ("What would you like your [] to do?"), whether they expect their eldest children to get married in the next 2 years, and how much dowry they expect to receive (or give). Because the expectations from both parents are highly correlated 4 and not all households identify/include a father, we use the expectations from mothers for the analysis. Note that the questions asked to the participants capture expectations rather than aspirations. Expectations about occupations are organized into 42 types of responses, which are then classified into 4 categories depending on the potential years of education that are required: i) "don't know" (which could mean the parents haven't thought about their children's future occupations, they don't see it as in their control, or they don't want to tell the interviewer;
ii) Traditional, or occupations that do not require high school. These occupations are mostly onfarm for girls (housewife, household land cultivation) and off the farm for boys; iii) Aspirational, for occupations that require a high school diploma, mainly government jobs, teachers, and professionals; iv) other occupations, which are ambiguous in terms of their school requirements, such as sales, NGOs, or private employees; large scale business, etc.

Our dataset is restricted to the subsample that contains information about expectations (around 87% of the initial dataset). One important element to highlight is the difference between the sizes of the treatment and control group. The total sample size is 7,788 observations, including both groups and the three survey waves. Out of this sample, 63% (4,938) of the observations belong to the treatment group and 37% (2,850) to the control group foot_7 . There are also significant differences between the characteristics of the treatment and control groups in the baseline (see table 1 .2). For example, there is a higher prevalence of parents from the treatment group who expect their eldest son will have a traditional occupation, although this difference is not significant for girls. Similarly, a larger percentage of girls from the treatment group are expected to have aspirational occupations, whereas a higher percentage in the control group expect them to have "other" types of occupations. Our empirical strategy (difference-in-differences), described in the methodology section, allows us to account for these discrepancies between the treatment and control group in the baseline.
The estimations follow a standard difference-in-difference (DiD) approach, used in other research papers with the same dataset (Bandiera et al., 2017 , Sulaiman, 2015) . We run separate regressions by gender-age group. The age groups correspond to primary school (6 to 10) and secondary school (11 to 15) (Sulaiman, 2015) . Additional adjustments include deleting households whose main respondent varied between the survey rounds, as these respondents can provide different expectations, and removing from the sample children whose gender changed or whose ages changed by more than 3 years between the survey waves because those are likely due to data entry errors foot_8 . Private tutoring is a popular practice in Bangladesh, reinforced by large class sizes and inadequate infrastructure. This practice is more frequent among wealthier households (Hasnat, 2017) . Child marriage does not seem to be a major cause of dropping out from school in this study because only 1.7% of the girls aged 12 to 17 dropped out from school due to marriage. Additionally, while child marriage seems to be prevalent in our study (around 9.6% of the girls aged 12 to 17 were married), the intervention did not have any effect on the probability of being married 9 . Regarding the effect on expectations, the BRAC-TUP intervention only exhibits significant effects for boys at reducing the aspirational gap, using both multinomial and binary outcomes (figure 1.2). In this section, we compare the changes in expectations between the baseline and the two follow-ups. We use the difference in the transition matrices for expectations between the treatment and control groups, disaggregated by gender. In this way, positive (negative) values indicate that a type of transition is more frequently found in the treatment (control) group. Note that the estimations included here are not causal, nor do they use any confidence intervals to measure whether the differences are significant. Additionally, there are some small differences in the distribution of the parental expectations about occupations at the baseline between the treatment and control groups. Nevertheless, differences that are close to zero indicate that the transitions between the treatment and control groups are relatively similar.
Our analysis shows that there are noticeable differences in the patterns found for boys and girls. For example, it is more likely for boys in the treatment group to move from traditional to aspirational occupations compared to the control group (see figure 1 .3). Likewise, parents who expected their sons to have aspirational occupations at the baseline are more likely to keep the same expectations. These patterns are visible for both follow-ups. These transitions explain why the effects of the intervention are positive for boys. For girls, the transitions exhibit significant differences between the first and second follow-ups. For the first follow up, there are two opposing effects: on the one hand, treated households who had aspirational expectations at the baseline are more likely to keep these expectations at the first follow up. This effect might be strengthened by parents in the treatment group who reported "don't know" at the baseline switching to aspirational at the first follow-up. On the other hand, treated households with traditional expectations for their daughters are less likely to move away from these expectations. Thus, it seems that the treatment reinforces the expectations that households had at the baseline, either traditional or aspirational.
As traditional expectations encompass around 50% and there are not significant differences between treatment and control at the baseline for this category, this matrix could explain why the net effect is close to zero. For the second follow up, traditional expectations are "reinforced" in the treatment group. However, treated households with unknown or aspirational expectations are less likely to switch to traditional expectations compared to the control group.
We can conclude from this section that the main difference between boys and girls is that for the former, treated households are more likely to switch to (or remain at) aspirational expectations. For girls, there are two opposing effects. While for some households the intervention reinforced the traditional gender roles for women, who are expected to perform household chores, for some others it allowed them to have aspirational expectations for their daughters.
We analyze whether the intervention has effects on enrollment, drop-out rates, and the likelihood of hiring a private tutor, and whether there are heterogenous effects based on the expectations reported at the baseline. In line with previous research (see Sulaiman, 2015) , we do not find any effects on enrollment or drop-out rates, nor are there heterogenous effects based on the expectations at the baseline. However, we find positive and significant effects of the intervention on the likelihood of hiring a private tutor for girls, and the effect is only significant for those parents who had aspirational occupations at the baseline. Figure 1 .4a) shows that girls aged 6 to 10 from treated households with aspirational expectations are 27% more likely to hire a private tutor after 2 years, and around 40% more likely to do so 4 years after the intervention. Note that the effects are only significant for girls in primary education, for which enrollment rates are high in Bangladesh. As we discuss in the robustness check, these results do not hold for other empirical specifications, so the results should be regarded with caution. Putting these results together, it seems that closing the gap in terms of expectations is not translated into improvements in observable outcomes (i.e., enrollment), and in the few cases where there are significant effects, these are only applicable to children in primary school (ages 6 to 10).
Although these results might sound pessimistic, there is evidence that treated households increased their expenditures on children's education (Sulaiman, 2015) . While we find the same pattern in our dataset, we do not analyze expenditures in education in more detail, as expenditures are calculated at the household level and not separately for each child. Additionally, we evaluate whether the program reduced the likelihood of children to drop out due to financial reasons (cannot afford expenses, or children must workoutside or at home -). We contrast these effects with dropping out voluntarily, as children state that the main reason for not going to school is because they do not want to, as in the case the intervention could have created other job opportunities that pulled children out of school. The results suggest that the intervention did not have significant effects at reducing dropping out rates due to financial reasons ( see figure 1.4b) ). Overall, the intervention had minor effects on educational outcomes.
There are three potential channels through which the BRAC-TUP intervention can affect participants' expectations: wealth effects, changes in in the valuation of education, and substitution effects. These channels are linked to the various components of the intervention. It is worth highlighting that it is not possible to disentangle the components of the program from one another, as the beneficiaries of the intervention receive all of them. Consequently, we cannot determine the magnitude of the effect for each of these channels. Our approach is simply to analyze which channels might be more relevant at explaining changes in expectations.
The literature shows that in general cash transfers (either conditional or unconditional) are associated with increases in aspirations related to income, assets, and social status (Mahli, 2020), and years of education and educational attainment (Gebremariam, Lodigiani, & Pasini, 2017; Cirillo, 2019; Garcia, Harker, & Ricaurte, 2019) . Additionally, La Ferrara (2019) finds positive effects of a graduation program on parents' expectations about children, particularly related to increases in the expected age of marriage for women. The only exception we noticed to these findings is Contreras Suarez and Cameron (2016) , who find that the program Familias en Acci√≥n, a conditional cash transfer program in Colombia, did not have any effect of changing the aspirations of the participants. In fact, the authors find that parents of beneficiary children believed that they were less likely to finish tertiary education than parents whose children did not benefit from the program. In our study, the wealth effect is generated by both the asset transferred and the stipend that beneficiary households receive.
Lack of income has been linked to changes in individuals' perceptions that could hinder their ability to invest in a better future as shown by literature pointing to the negative effects that lack of income has over individuals' motivation and stress levels (Mahli, 2017) . Individuals with higher stress levels are more prone to short-sighted behaviors and are more risk-averse, leading them to stop investing in asset accumulation or education. Therefore, the provision of assistance in the form of cash or in-kind transfers, as well as assets, could help them by reducing the stress caused by the uncertainty caused by the unsteady income (Mahli, 2017) and inability to smooth consumption (Gebremariam et al, 2017) . Transfers also improve the psychological well-being of participants by providing them with financial security that enables them to regain control of their lives and fulfill their expectations (Samuels & Stavropoulou, 2016) , or by providing them with symbolic needs, such as new uniforms or better meals (Adato, Devereux, & Sabates-Wheeler, 2016) . Transfers can also increase trust in the government, as individuals perceive that they have access to a safety net (Mahli, 2017) .
In the poverty literature, wealth effects are also linked to the existence of poverty traps. In a recent paper by Balboni et al. (2022) , the authors estimate the cutoff for classifying households into a poverty trap using the same context as ours (BRAC-TUP in Bangladesh). The authors find that those households that were not pushed far enough from the poverty trap after receiving the intervention return to their low initial levels of consumption, and do not "graduate" from poverty.
Related research has found that the benefits of the BRAC-TUP intervention do not spread to all participants (Bandiera et al., 2017 , Misha, Raza, & Ara, 2019) . Thus, these households who did not benefit could face the same conditions that limit perceptions described above, such as higher levels of stress and short-sighted behaviors, among others.
Here, we explore wealth effects by separating households into two levels of wealth, consistent with how poverty traps operate, as well as by analyzing the effects that the intervention had on some of the variables related to the lack of income: anxiety, discount rates, and risk attitudes. For this purpose, we split the sample into 2 groups (outside the poverty trap = 1, in the poverty trap= 0) using the cutoff estimated by Balboni et al (2022) for the BRAC-TUP setting. We measure this cutoff at the first follow up because at the baseline only a small percentage of the households were above the poverty trap. Additionally, because if there exists a wealth effect, it should be stronger at the first follow-up, when households already received the asset and are able to move over the poverty trap. Discount rates are measured using two questions asked to the main female respondent, "Would you like to receive 200 takas (BTK) today or 250 in 1 month?", and for those who chose the option that gives 250 in the future, the next question asks, "How many more months would you be able to wait to receive 250?" Based on these questions, we construct a discount rate that measures the number of months households would be willing to wait to receive 50 extra takas. It is important to highlight that 200 BKT in 2007 were equivalent to around USD$25 in PPP 14 . This is a significant amount, considering that the poverty line was established in USD$1.25 per person per day for 2007 (Bandiera et. Al, 2017) . We do not find significant effects of the intervention on discount rates.
We do not find effects of the intervention on risk attitudes or anxiety. To measure risk attitudes, participants are asked during the two follow-ups (and not at the baseline) to choose between four hypothetical lotteries, each one with two outcomes with equal probabilities (win/lose). Lottery one pays 100 BTK for both outcomes; lottery 2 pays 200 if winning and 60 if losing; lottery 3 pays 300 and 20; and lottery 4 pays 400 and 0 respectively. Although lottery 4 has the highest expected outcome (200), it is also the riskiest. Overall, households rank lottery 4 first (40%), followed by lottery 1 (36%), lottery 2 (14%), and lottery 1 (10%). We measure anxiety based on the question "Did you suffer any mental anxiety that hampered your daily activities last month?", and we select those households who reported that the most important reason for anxiety is "financial scarcity at home." Other reasons for anxiety, such as quarrels with spouses, concerns about children, among others, were not considered. In general, more than 63% of the households reported feeling anxious and 46% were anxious due to economic reasons.
Regarding happiness, we find that treated households were more likely to answer that they consider their lives as "happy" or "very happy". At the baseline, most of the ultra-poor households answered that they felt unhappy with their lives (59%). After the first follow-up, this percentage decreased to 43% and remained around the same (41%) for the second follow-up foot_12 . These results are in line with qualitative research conducted for the same population, which shows that participants' levels of happiness increased with the intervention (Roy et al, 2015; Kabeer, 2019) 16 .
Additionally, we explore whether happier households are more likely to have aspirational expectations about children using a triple D-in-D approach. We estimate positive effects on happiness attributable to the intervention (See Table 1 .3). However, we do not find heterogenous effects on expectations for children based on the level of happiness reported by the households at the baseline.
Overall, we do not find that the intervention had any effects on the variables associated with the lack of income. This could be a result of the low levels of income of the population targeted by the intervention, as even after receiving the treatment these households remain relatively poor compared to the average in Bangladesh. It could also indicate that the effects of the program were not large enough to produce changes in these variables.
Despite these results, we cannot rule out wealth effects, nor can we argue that increases in income do not have any effects on educational outcomes. For example, there is evidence that treated households increased their expenditures on education relative to the control group (Bandiera et al, 2017; Sulaiman, 2015) . However, changes in income might not be the main driver for changes in expectations. For this reason, the results are similar for both groups of wealth. One potential explanation is that for households to have higher expectations about their children's future it is necessary to have a minimum level of income. However, once that level of income is reached, other components, such as access to information, education, or the existence of role models can shape those expectations more finely. Additionally, the BRAC-TUP intervention has different components that act in conjunction with each other. Hence, changes in the mindset of participants (for example, through coaching) can only be observable after the material needs are fulfilled (through the provision of the asset or the monthly stipend).
Although international evidence suggests that more productive assets in the household are correlated with better educational outcomes (Ansong et al, 2019) , some studies have found negative effects. For example, Kafle, Jollife, and Winter-Nelson (2018) find that the acquisition of agricultural assets reduces school enrolment in Tanzania, and the effect is larger for children of crop producers in rural areas. These effects are the result of a tradeoff between hours studying and hours working with the productive asset. Thus, as the BRAC-TUP intervention seeks to increase agricultural asset ownership among beneficiaries, if receiving these assets forces children to reduce the hours dedicated to their studies or to drop out of school, the intervention would have negative effects on educational outcomes. This could be explained by the expected net returns of sending children to school being offset by the gains of children exploiting the asset and spending more time in household production (Cockburn & Dostie, 2007) . These results could be reinforced if the expectations about children are traditional, or if they switch to traditional. For example, if parents perceive that agricultural activities could be viable long-term occupations to secure children's future wellbeing. Similarly, because some parents in rural areas express concerns about sending their daughters to school due to security reasons, girls could be more prone to remain at home involved in livestock activities after receiving the asset (Hasnat, 2017) . The availability of productive assets changes the cost/benefit calculation of sending children, and especially girls, to school.
Because our dataset does not contain information about the hours children devote to their studies, it is not possible to calculate a tradeoff between livestock activities and time studying.
Instead, we take a different approach. First, we evaluate whether changes in the time spent in livestock activities are large enough to offset the time children spend studying. Then, we analyze whether livestock activities rely mostly on the adult members of the household, particularly women, or on children. Regarding the former item, Sulaiman (2015) found that the BRAC-TUP intervention increased the yearly hours in livestock activities after 2 years between 33 and 35 hours for boys and girls aged 6 to 10, and by around 122 hours for the age group 11 to 15 for both boys and girls. This means that on average, there are no significant differences between boys and girls, and the increases are less than 1 hour per week for younger children, and less than 2.5 hours per

Our estimations are similar but larger in magnitude than Sulaiman's, considering that we use a subsample comprised only by the eldest children within each household. As robustness checks, we conduct two types of exercises. First, we show that the estimations are robust to the inclusion of additional covariates and modifications to the outcome variable. We exclude the category "don't know" from the sample and re-run the estimations. Then, we include a set of covariates related to how close (in terms of travel time) households are to the nearest schools (primary, high school, and college), health centers, bazaars, and paved roads 21 .
Second, we used more flexible DID specifications to calculate the main results. We use the Doubly Robust DID estimation (DRDID) proposed by Sant'Anna and Zhao (2020) and the Double/debiased Machine Learning (DMLDiD) by Chang (2020) . We do this for two reasons.
First, DID assumes parallel trends. Because we only have one pre-intervention period, it is not possible to empirically test whether this assumption holds, and although the data come from an RCT, the descriptive statistics show significant discrepancies between the treated and control groups at the baseline. These methods assume that the parallel trends hold but conditional on a set of characteristics (i.e., different trends for boys and girls), which is a less restrictive assumption.
Second, the set of covariates includes both time-variant and time-invariant characteristics, and some of them could be influenced by the intervention. For example, school enrollment could be affected by the intervention, but at the same time parental expectations might change depending on whether children go to school. The robust methods control only by the set of covariates that are time invariant or were collected at the baseline. In this sense, we check whether the results are robust to characteristics that cannot be influenced by the program. We provide a more detailed explanation of the methods in appendix A.
The results show that adding covariates related to distances (in time) to schools, hospitals, and other important places does not change the estimations (either in direction or magnitude). In this sense, the expectations about children do not change by how far away households are located from educational centers. On the other hand, removing answers coded as "don't know" from the sample changes the significance of the estimations, but only for girls. In this sense, the effect of the intervention is now negative and significant for aspirational occupations. There are no significant changes that result from removing the "don't know" answers for boys. The estimations with different empirical specifications are not disaggregated by age groups. Because these methods rely on an inverse probability matching, a more extensive set of covariates and smaller groups (more disaggregated) can cause the propensity score not to converge. The results show that in general, independent of the empirical specification, the intervention increased aspirational expectations and reduced traditional expectations for boys. For girls on the other hand, aspirational expectations remained constant in the first follow up, but the DMLDiD estimations show decreases in aspirational expectations for the second follow up, which is in line with omitting "don't know" from the sample. Overall, our results confirm differences by gender. While aspirational expectations increased for boys, they remained the same or decreased for girls (see figure 1.8 and table 1.6).

to the different empirical specifications (see table 1.7 ). Finally, we analyze whether the effect of the intervention on hiring a private tutor is robust to the different specifications. For example, none of the estimations are significantly different from zero using DRDID, but the DMLDiD models show positive and significant effects (at 5% level) of the intervention for boys and girls for the second follow up only.

Although increases in the probability of hiring a private tutor might reflect beneficiary households allocating more resources to education (Sulaiman, 2015; Bandiera et al., 2017) , the results could reflect heterogenous effects that are not captured by the variables used in our estimations.
In this research, we analyze whether parental expectations about occupations are affected by the BRAC graduation program -Targeting the Ultra-poor (TUP) in Bangladesh. We then explore potential mechanisms that could explain these changes in expectations, namely wealth and substitution effects, and changes in the valuation of education. Additionally, we link these changes in expectations to enrollment, drop-out, and investments in education, exploring whether higher expectations about children are translated into better educational outcomes. Our findings suggest that the intervention reduces the aspirational gap that exists between the ultra-poor and wealthier households, but the effects are only applicable to boys. These results are robust to changes in the empirical specification, the introduction of additional control variables, and changes in the definition of the outcome variable. One potential explanation is that the intervention might not be challenging traditional gender roles. Even though this is not an objective sought by the intervention, whose purpose focuses on "graduating" households from poverty, the intervention could benefit from strengthening the gender component. In this regard, Roy et al. (2015) finds that while the beneficiary women from BRAC-TUP held control over the transferred assets, they experienced losses in terms of mobility and decision-making over other household assets. Thus, these results suggest that the benefits of the program might differ by gender. Another way in which the program might reinforce traditional gender roles is that by increasing farm income, it encourages parents to see the agricultural housewife role as an economically viable one in the future.
Our research also highlights the importance of the coaching component for complex interventions, such as "graduation" programs. Considering that the intervention has different components that complement each other, it is not possible to determine the contribution of each component to the outcomes analyzed. However, we show that the intervention improved households' knowledge in topics covered during the life skill training/coaching sessions, such as political awareness and health awareness. We also find that non-eligible poor households in treated villages also benefit from the intervention (spillover effect), as a higher proportion of the households expect their children to have aspirational occupations relative to the control villages.
Because these households did not receive assets or other monetary transfers from the intervention, the effect should be attributed to the non-tangible components of the program, such as the coaching component. Recent literature shows the importance of this component on poverty alleviation interventions and the spillovers it generates (Roelen & Deveraux, 2019; Banerjee et al., 2022) . We cannot rule out the role of mechanisms such as wealth effects, even though both the "poorest" and "better-off" ultra-poor households (as well as those above and below the poverty trap cutoff) experienced similar effects in terms of expectations.
While the intervention shows positive effects on expectations, these results do not translate directly into improvements in educational outcomes, nor are there heterogeneous effects based on the expectations that parents have about their children. We only found positive and significant effects of the intervention on the likelihood of hiring a private teacher for girls in primary school for those parents with aspirational expectations. However, these effects are not robust to the different specifications. Previous research did not find significant effects of the program on educational outcomes other than expenditures on education (Sulaiman 2015; Bandiera et al., 2017) .
These results might indicate few effects of the intervention on changing the participants' mindsets, or that the effects of the intervention beyond the initial objectives ("graduating households from poverty") are small or not immediate. We hope that this study motivates further research on the non-economic and unintended effects of antipoverty interventions. In this sense, if these interventions can generate changes among the beneficiaries that go beyond providing productive assets and how to use them properly, perhaps these households could become less vulnerable to poverty, even if they lose the assets. Similarly, if these interventions could reinforce gender disparities, this type of research could help policy makers to take preventive actions before implementing these interventions. Soybean is one of the most important crops in Brazil, not only for its significant contribution to the economy, but also for playing a major role in the development of the agricultural sector in the country. Soybean was introduced in Brazil by Japanese immigrants in 1882 to produce tofu and soy sauce (Martinelli, Batistella, Silva, & Moran, 2017) . However, the production of soybean at large scale in the country began in the 1950s (Vander Vennet, Schneider, & Dessein, 2016) , when farmers from Rio Grande do Sul grew the grain as a fertilizer for wheat to fix nitrogen in the soil (de Souza & Busch, 1998) . This production was initially developed in the Southern region, characterized by a temperate climate. The production then expanded to the West of the country in the seventies (Mato Grosso and Goi√°s), and it continued its expansion to the North. This latter expansion was promoted by the Brazilian government with the aid of Embrapa (Brazilian Agricultural Research Corporation), which played a major role in the development of new soy varietals for different climates and soil conditions in the Brazilian regions, as well in promoting the use of limestone in the Cerrado biome to correct the soil pH and make it suitable for agriculture (Cunha & Espƒ± ≈Ñdola, 2015) . In the past 30 years, states such as Maranh√£o, Tocantins, Piau√≠, Par√°, Amap√°, and Rond√¥nia began producing soybean (Martinelli, Batistella, Silva, & Moran, 2017) . The production in these states is referred to as the expansion of soybean in Brazil, as these states comprise the new agricultural producers in the country (see figure 2.1).
The expansion of soybean in Brazil was mainly led by farmers coming from southern states (Rio Grande do Sul, Santa Catarina, and Paran√°), and the production targeted the foreign markets (Goldsmith & Hirsch, 2006; Choi & Kim, 2016) . These farmers benefitted from low prices of land, improvements in the transportation infrastructure, favorable external prices, and access to government programs that aimed to expand the agricultural frontier in Brazil. Additionally, these farmers had access to the financial market and experience in the production of soybean, which contrasted with the households inhabiting the new agricultural areas, characterized by low-income levels and economies mostly based on livestock (Choi & Kim, 2016) . The expansion also helped Brazil to become the largest producer of soybeans in the world, reflecting the importance of this crop for the Brazilian economy. The production increased from 566 million bushels in 1991 to 4,965 million bushels in 2021 (Colussi & Schnitkey, 2021) Despite this successful growth, the soybean expansion in Brazil is sometimes labelled as a bad example of development, mainly due to its environmental effects (Vander Vennet, Schneider, & Dessein, 2016; Russo Lopes, Bastos Lima, & dos Reis, 2021) . Initially occupying degraded pastures (Gomes, De Lima, Teixeira Gomes, & Teixeira Filho, 1991) , soybean farmers began to deforesting zones of Cerrado and Amazon biomes generating negative environmental impacts (Ioris, 2017) . Some scholars argue that the deforestation of the Brazilian Amazon is in part caused by sociodemographic changes induced by the expansion of soybean. For example, Weinhold, Killick, and Reis (2013) argue that the expansion caused increases in prices (including land) and encouraged low-income farmers to sell their lands and migrate north, where prices are lower, but the lands are mostly covered by forests and not adapted to agricultural production. Other authors suggest that the patterns of deforestation vary among regions, with farmers who moved from the South and have inhabited the Amazon for a longer time being the major actors in generating deforestation (Caviglia-Harris, Sills, & Mullan, 2013) . In recent years, the Brazilian government has enacted new policies to prevent deforestation by forcing farmers to preserve a percentage of the land in forests, and this percentage depends on the biome foot_19 . Nevertheless, the effect of these policies has been limited to preserve biodiversity in some soybean-producing areas (Polizel, et al., 2021) Although the impacts of the soybean expansion on deforestation are well documented, the literature about its socioeconomic effects is more limited. Some scholars point to the positive effects of the expansion, as it brings increases in productivity and welfare due to the economic growth in the region, which could improve the diversification of local economies (Weinhold, Killick, & Reis, 2013) . In fact, although the production of soybean is capital intensive in these regions, Richards, Pellegrina, VanWey, and Spera (2015) find that regions with soybean production in Mato Grosso have experienced significant growth in non-agricultural sectors, such as services, commerce, construction, education, and health. This diversification is linked to the development of poultry, hog, and beef industries, as well as cooking oil industries in Brazil (Warnken, 2000) . It is also linked to the transformation of small cities into prosperous regional centers, where the grains are used in agro-industrial clusters or stored in warehouses and then exported to other countries (Siani & Hayashi, 2021; Oliveira & Santana, 2012; Brum, Dalfovo, & Azuaga, 2009; de Oliveira, D√∂rner, & Schneider, 2020) . Consequently, indicators such as the Gross Domestic Product (GDP) per capita, employment rates, and measures of development (i.e.
the Human Development Index -HDI) in soybean municipalities had better performance than nonsoybean municipalities (Brum, Dalfovo, & Azuaga, 2009; Orlandi, Willers, Staduto, Eberhardt, & Piacenti, 2012; VanWey, Spera, de Sa, Mahr, & Mustard, 2013; Lima-de-Oliveira & Alonso, 2017; da Silva Filho, Pereira, & Miyamoto, 2020) .
Despite these positive outcomes, researchers have also found negative effects attributed to the expansion of soybean. Episodes of conflicts, violence, and tension directed against small farmers to force them away from their lands were common in the areas of analysis (Heredia, Palmeira, and Leite 2010) . Although the Brazilian government enacted policies to protect the small farmers in 1990s, these policies could not stop the migration of small farmers to the cities' slums, which exposed them to multiple forms of economic, food, and physical insecurity (Russo Lopes, Bastos Lima, & dos Reis, 2021). Similarly, scholars find increases in income inequality (Weinhold, Killick, & Reis, 2013; Almeida & de Mattos Junior, 2016; Bolfe, et al., 2016; Pereira, Porcionato, & de Castro, 2018) , and mixed findings regarding poverty. While Choi and Kim (2016) found increases in poverty in the North, Weinhold, Killick, and Reis (2013) find the opposite effects in the Amazon. Other negative effects include water pollution due to the use of agrochemicals in the production of soybean; reductions in the variety of crops, as some crops are negatively affected by pesticides; and increased gender disparities, as most of the jobs created by the soybean complex are for men (Russo Lopes, Bastos Lima, & dos Reis, 2021 ).
An important limitation of these studies is that the estimated effects cannot be causally attributed to the expansion of soybean. Some of these studies are qualitative and intend to measure the perception of the respondents regarding the expansion of soybean (Russo Lopes, Bastos Lima, & dos Reis, 2021), Similarly, most of the quantitative studies are descriptive or use correlational analysis to compare municipalities on the basis of the amount of soybean produced (Weinhold, Killick, & Reis, 2013; Martinelli, Batistella, Silva, & Moran, 2017) or compare soybean to other economic activities, particularly sugarcane and livestock (Garrett & Rausch, 2016) . Only two of the studies analyzed use causal inference techniques, more specifically difference-in-differences: Choi and Kim (2016) and Bragan√ßa (2018) . The former uses census data for 1991 and 2000, not considering the expansion that took place in the North in the early 2000s. Bragan√ßa (2018) is the only study that measures the effects of the soybean expansion in the North using yearly municipal GDP data between 1999 and 2012, combined with census data for the years 2000 and 2010 to measure households' changes in assets and access to public goods.
The second limitation is the selection of municipalities that comprise the comparison group, as most of the studies analyzed include all the municipalities in Brazil that do not grow soybean. The only exception is Bragan√ßa (2018) , who selects municipalities located outside of the Cerrado biome as the control group. These latter municipalities are unlikely to produce soybeans due to their geographical conditions. The areas producing soybeans in Brazil share common geographical and socioeconomic characteristics. The production is concentrated in clusters of contiguous municipalities (Santos de Ara√∫jo, et al., 2019) , and these clusters vary in terms of the socioeconomic conditions and the type of production, with the production in the South characterized by small farms, higher crop diversification, higher rural labor employment and higher soybean yield (Bicudo da Silva, et al., 2020). Hence, the inclusion of all Brazilian municipalities in the comparison group (or all the municipalities producing soybeans in the treatment group) and assigning them homogeneous weights (as in correlational studies or difference-in-differences) can lead to biases, as these areas can be significantly different in terms of development. Similarly, for the case of difference-in-differences, it is difficult to satisfy the parallel trend assumption, especially when there is no continuous data available (as in the case of decennial census data).
A third limitation is that the adoption of soybean occurred at different times between and within each of the regions in Brazil (Vander Vennet, Schneider, & Dessein, 2016; de Oliveira, D√∂rner, & Schneider, 2020) . To our knowledge, no other study has considered a staggered setting to analyze the adoption of soybean production at the municipal level.
This research analyzes the impacts of the recent expansion of soybean in Brazil within each municipality on formal employment. Our methodological approach addresses the limitations described before. We use a Synthetic Control Matching (SCM) methodology following Abadie and Gardeazabal (2003) and Abadie, Diamond, Hainmueller (2010; 2015) , as well as recent approaches by Xu (2017) and Ben-Michael, Feller, and Rothstein (2021) . This methodology does not require strong assumptions such as parallel trends, nor does it assign the same weight to every observation in the control group, which makes the results highly susceptible to the selection of the control municipalities. Our analysis focuses on the causal effects for the municipalities affected by the expansion and not their neighbors. Thus, the introduction of space into the analysis could be a topic for further research. Nevertheless, we use some tools provided by the regional economics literature, such as spatial correlations, and the use of areas of influence, a concept we describe in more detail in the following sections, to analyze how our selected municipalities evolve relative to other neighboring municipalities.
As the production of soybeans in each municipality cannot be regarded as a "treatment", we develop an identification strategy to circumvent this limitation. In this way, we define the municipalities affected by the expansion as those that experienced significant and rapid increases in the production of soybeans between 2005 and 2014. The years when these increases occurred denote a structural break in the production within each municipality and are defined as the "treatment year". In fact, we show that these increases in the production of soybeans led to significant reductions in the crop diversity index within the "treated" municipalities. The control set is composed by the municipalities that have the geographic conditions to grow soybeans but did not produce significant amounts in the period analyzed. This approach differs from papers such as Bragan√ßa (2018) , where the comparison group is composed by municipalities that have a high percentage of the area (50% or more) covered by Caatinga, a semi-arid biome not entirely apt for agriculture on a large commercial basis. Instead, we let the municipalities in the comparison group have adequate conditions for the development of agricultural production, making them more comparable to areas where the soybean production thrives. Finally, SCM allows us to estimate effects by cohorts, acknowledging the staggered adoption of soybeans in the municipalities. It is important to highlight that the expansion of soybean has occurred from the South to the North of the country, and it has been driven by geographical conditions and the suitability of soils for agriculture rather than by socioeconomic variables (see figure B.1). Therefore, the set of control municipalities are mostly located in the northernmost area of analysis.
We present two types of estimations: aggregated effects of the expansion for the group of treated municipalities, and heterogeneous effects by group, focusing on those municipalities for which we might expect larger effects from the expansion. For the latter exercise, we analyze i) the municipalities that had the largest investments, measured in terms of machinery and financial resources received (from both private and the government) in per capita terms, ii) the intensity in the production of soybeans, iii) the percentage of formal jobs in agriculture in these municipalities, as measured by the 2010 Brazilian census; and iv) the municipalities that have on average the largest values for the previous three categories. Our hypothesis is that if there are heterogeneities in the estimated effects, in municipalities where the expansion led to higher production (intensity), received higher investments, and had a larger formal sector, the effects of the expansion in terms formal jobs might be higher.
This paper contributes to the scant literature about the causal effects of the soybean expansion on socioeconomic variables. We analyze whether the expansion is linked to the diversification of the municipal economies that comprise the agricultural frontier in Brazil, as some studies claim but did not show causally. Hence, we estimate whether the expansion allowed municipalities to create more formal jobs in key sectors other than agriculture, such as industry and services. Additionally, because our study focuses on the agricultural frontier rather than on all the municipalities producing soybean, it could point to potential effects of the expansion in areas where soybean production has not expanded yet. We hope that our methodological framework could be used for analyzing other outcomes variables when longer data series are available. Finally, our paper introduces some elements from the regional economics literature to provide a better context of the causal effects estimated.
We focus our analysis on formal jobs for various reasons. Because the region of analysis is characterized by large farms and a highly mechanized production, it is relevant to understand to what extent increases in the production of soybeans translate into more quality jobs. The region of analysis has on average some of the highest rates of informality in the country. For the 2010 Brazilian census, states like Maranh√£o, Piau√≠, and Tocantins had formal rates of employment lower than 30%, whereas the rates for other states involved in the production of soybeans, such as Santa Catarina, Rio Grande do Sul, and Paran√°, were above 45% 23 . Informal employees do not benefit from access to a pension, paid vacation, or unemployment insurance. Moreover, the informal sector does not contribute to the social insurance system, which reduces the social benefits provided by the government. In this sense, our work also adds to the analysis of whether the soybean expansion could be contributing to the reduction of informality in the area. Further, the creation of formal jobs could also help in the reduction of inequalities in the region, a recurrent topic found in previous studies. One element to highlight is that the formal jobs analyzed in this research are measured based on the sectors firms are assigned to, independent of their employees' occupations. In this way, jobs in agriculture could include occupations other than farming, such as drivers or engineers, if they work for a company in the agricultural sector. However, we also analyze whether there are increases in formal jobs in other sectors, as the expansion could create linkages with these sectors and dynamize the economy 24 .

We focus on the socioeconomic effects of the expansion of soybean. Thereby, we define as treated those municipalities where the production of soybean has increased rapidly and significantly. It should be noted that the municipalities selected represent an optimal situation for determining the upper limits of soybean production effects, since rapid changes in the production allow for less time to anticipate and strategically determine the appropriate number of hires. As a result, we anticipate greater employment growth in these cases compared to scenarios in which soybean production increases gradually over time. We define significant production as those municipalities that produced at least 60,000 tons of soybeans in 2018 foot_23 . To define rapid expansion, we focus on changes in the production trends. Based on the production series, we identify whether the production of soybean in each municipality has kinks, where the trend in the production changed. We define this kink as the year when the municipality was labeled as "treated" (see figure 2 .2). Out of the 1,610 municipalities in the region of analysis, 188 produced more than 60,000 tons of soybeans at some point between 1996 and 2018. Out of these 188 municipalities, all of them have less than 30% of the area with Caatinga, 99.5% had more than 900mm of precipitation per year, 87.2% had mostly flat lands and 92.5% adequate soils. Thus, 156 of these municipalities (83.0%) satisfied the four conditions, 24 (12.8%) had three conditions and only 8 (4.3%) had two conditions.
To complete the sample selection, we consider municipalities with 5,000 or more inhabitants in 2010, and we restrict the treated municipalities to those that were "treated" prior to 2014. The first condition is used as unpopulated municipalities are unlikely to have linkage effects, which is one of the main outcomes in this research (as measured through the creation of formal jobs and changes in the municipal GDP). The second condition is used to have a window of at least 4 years to evaluate the effects of the expansion of the last treated units. In total, the sample comprises 39 treated municipalities and 262 municipalities in the donor pool (control group) 28 .
Regarding our outcome variables, jobs are categorized depending on the sector of the firm.
In this way, if a firm is labeled as "agricultural", it means that all the reported jobs within that firm are agricultural jobs, independent of whether some of these positions are for engineers, salesmen, etc. Based on this classification, we create two distinct categories of jobs: direct and indirect. Direct indicates the jobs that are directly affected by the expansion of soybean, which includes agricultural jobs, as well as those belonging to the soybean value chain. Our main empirical strategy is Synthetic Control Matching (SCM), originally proposed by Abadie and Gardeazabal (2003) . This methodology creates a control unit based on the weighted average of the donor units to best resemble the behavior of the treatment unit during the pretreatment period. Compared to other techniques designed for non-experimental data, SCM makes the selection of the control units more transparent, as these are selected based on the pre-treatment characteristics and the weights assigned to each donor unit are shown. Thereby, this selection is not affected by future outcomes (Abadie, 2021) . Similarly, SCM precludes extrapolation, which means that the technique will use the most similar control units to calculate the effect by restricting the weights assigned to each donor unit to be non-negative and add up to 1. In other words, SCM calculates a counterfactual for each treated unit and each outcome variable using the observations from the donor set during the pre-treatment period. During this process, usually a sample of the units from the donor set (and not all of them) receive a weight greater than zero.

Apart from the established functional forms, the models analyzed have some implicit assumptions. First, they assume that there is no serial autocorrelation of the error term. In cases of strong serial dependence, such as unit root processes, the models will not provide reliable estimations. Thus, the standard error must have mean zero. Second, the assignment to treatment can depend on pre-treatment outcomes, but it cannot depend on post-treatment outcomes.

It is worth noting that the magnitude of the estimated effects using SCM also depends on the set of control municipalities. This research analyzes the effects of the soybean expansion between 1996 and 2018 at the agricultural frontier of Brazil on formal jobs. The expansion is defined as a rapid and significant increase in the amount of soybean produced in each municipality. We use a synthetic control matching approach, addressing some of the shortcomings from the previous literature, specifically the estimation of causal effects rather than correlations, a careful selection of the control municipalities and their weights in the estimations, such that only municipalities that have the geographical conditions to produce soybeans are included, and the adoption of a method suitable to a setting with staggered treatment, acknowledging that the expansion occurred at different times across the Brazilian municipalities. We find that the expansion had some effects on the creation of formal jobs in agriculture (direct effects), but there is no evidence of multiplicative effects in other sectors. Additionally, we did not find evidence of substitutability between jobs in agriculture and livestock, at least not for formal jobs.
We then look for potential explanations for these results and for this purpose, we conduct two types of analyses. First, we use tools from the regional economics literature to compare the growth in formal jobs for the treated municipalities relative to other neighboring municipalities or municipalities that share frequent exchanges of goods and population flows (regions of influence).
Our results show that the municipalities defined as treated experienced growth in formal jobs in agriculture above average when compared to other municipalities that did not produce soybeans within the region of influence, but below average when compared to municipalities producing soybeans that were not included in the treatment group. These latter municipalities correspond mostly to well established producers for which we did not have a "pre-intervention" period and for this reason were not included in the estimations. We complement this regional analysis with the calculation of the Local Moran's I, to show that overall, neighbors of the treated municipalities experienced similar patterns to the treated municipalities. For example, those municipalities that experienced significant growth in jobs per thousand inhabitants are usually surrounded by other municipalities that also experienced similar increases. Although the analysis provided by the regional economics tools is not causal, we find it unlikely that the effects of the expansion are being absorbed by the neighboring municipalities.
We then complement the regional analysis with heterogenous estimations of the effects of the soybean expansion. We measure heterogeneity in terms of four characteristics: investments (both in terms of machinery and financial resources), the intensity of the production (in tons of soybeans produced), the size of the agricultural formal sector, and a combination of the previous three categories. We expect that municipalities with higher values in the previous three categories experienced larger effects on employment. Although we find that effects are larger for such municipalities, we do not find evidence of multiplicative effects of the expansion in sectors other than agriculture. Nonetheless, the effects in agricultural jobs are significant: for the municipalities with the highest values in the three categories, the average effect ranges between 35 and 46 jobs per 1,000 inhabitants. Considering that the average population in these municipalities is around 10,000, this means that the creation of more than 350 formal jobs can be attributed to the expansion of soybean production.
Based on our results, we believe that the municipalities analyzed seem to be specializing in production of agricultural staples, mainly soybeans, with little or no diversification to other sectors. We propose two potential explanations for this outcome. First, that the creation of linkages to other sectors takes time, and only the municipalities that started producing soybeans in the 1990s were able to develop economic sectors other than agriculture. Second, that the largest benefits to the expansion of soybeans are allocated to the first producers or the municipalities that first developed companies tied to the production of soybeans, either industrial or services. For example, we find that the group of municipalities composed by the large soybean producers that began their production in the 1990s had the highest increases in formal jobs per capita in the regions where our "treatment" municipalities are located.
Our analysis has limitations and potential extensions. The main limitations are the exclusion of informal employment, and the lack of information related to other socioeconomic indicators, such as health or education, that could complement the analysis. Hence, the only healthrelated variable available for the period of analysis was child mortality, which was highly volatile.
Information about educational outcomes, such as scores on standardized tests or enrollment, was only available from 2001. Further, the last census to this date in Brazil dates from 2010.
Nevertheless, with more years of data and the new census projected for 2022, it is possible to expand the study to other outcomes, including aspects such as internal migration. This research could also benefit from the inclusion of causal spatial effects. In this sense, although our estimations point to the creation of economies of specialization, our framework does not allow us to estimate whether the expansion of soybean contributed to increased economic specialization among the municipalities, or whether municipalities that were not directly affected by the expansion (i.e., did not produce soybeans) had any effects on jobs in sectors other than agriculture that could be a consequence of the expansion. Finally, considering that the literature presented in this research shows increases in inequality in the regions producing soybeans, it is worth analyzing what types of jobs were "created" with the expansion, and whether these jobs target the poorest households in the region. The literature about peer effects has shown that high-performing classmates can have benefits on students' outcomes, such as educational attainment and labor market achievement. Not surprisingly, parents choose high schools based on their reputation, the percentage of their students who go to selective colleges, and their students' results on standardized tests based on the belief that those selective schools will bring better outcomes for their children than an average high school. However, recent literature shows that the benefits of attending such schools could be offset when students perceive that many of their peers perform relatively better in class (low rankings); whereas students attending less competitive schools (or schools with lower-performing students) could benefit from being at the top of their classes in terms of performance (high rankings). This phenomenon, known in Psychology as the big fish in small pond effect (Marsh, 1987; Marsh, et al., 2008) , has received recent attention to explain labor and educational outcomes. This paper explores the potential effects of rankings on college dynamics.
Students with similar ability but higher school rankings develop higher self-perceived intelligence and self-confidence, which is positively correlated with non-cognitive skills. As a result, these students may be more disciplined and motivated and put more effort into their studies, translating to better academic results (Elsner & Isphording, 2017) . Conversely, there is evidence that the students with similar ability, but lower school rankings, develop lower academic selfconfidence in selective schools relative to nonselective schools. For instance, Hoekstra, Hoekstra, Mouganie, and Wang (2018) use surveys to show that some students experience negative effects from being exposed to higher competition in selective schools because it makes them feel academically inferior. Shi (2020) shows that higher competition among students in selective high schools increases the number of applications to selective colleges, but only students with high rankings apply to STEM fields because these fields are perceived to be reserved for the top students. Further, Houng and Ryan (2018) argue that for average-to low-ranked students the negative effects of rankings offset the benefits of attending selective high schools, which could explain why papers that use a Regression Discontinuity (RD) approach to measure the effect that attending such schools has on test scores do not find significant results (Abdulkadiroƒülu, Angrist, & Pathak, 2014; Dobbie & Fryer, 2014) . This literature also points to the relevance of measuring the effects not only on average but disaggregated by groups, such as low-performing and highperforming students.
The literature about ranking effects has focused mainly on pre-college education. The results from this literature suggest that students with higher school rankings but similar ability have better results on standardized tests in the long run, higher probabilities of receiving college admissions and finishing high school, and that these effects are attributed mostly to increases in the self-reported confidence and higher expectations of students (Murphy & Weinhardt, 2020; Yu, 2020; Elsner & Isphording, 2017) . Similarly, students with higher rankings are less prone to engage in risky behaviors (drinking, smoking, unprotected sex) and have better mental health indicators, as measured by the depression scale from the Center of Epidemiologic Studies (CES-D) (Elsner & Isphording, 2018; Kiessling & Norris, 2020) . These findings point to the long-lasting effects of rankings on students. Thus, it is not surprising that some colleges use school rankings to determine admissions. Nevertheless, there are two aspects that have not received much attention.
The first one is changes in rankings, particularly when students move from one educational cycle to another, such as high school to college. In other words, do students who were at the top of their classes in high school react in the same way as "average" students when both experience low performance levels in college, and are they more likely to persevere during adverse situations (such as failing courses)? Using Marsh's terminology, what happens when the big fish moves to a larger pond? The second aspect is related to what other information rankings might provide that could be useful for colleges in understanding adverse outcomes such as switching programs, failing courses, or delaying graduation.
There are three main objectives of this study. The first one is to analyze whether changes between high-school rankings and rankings based on the performance in the first semester of college affect educational outcomes at the college level. The big-fish-in-small-pond effect implies that top students from less competitive schools develop higher self-confidence than similar students in more competitive environments foot_32 . However, when these students transition to a more competitive environment, such as a selective university, it is likely that they might not be at the top of their classes. Moreover, it is possible that they fail some of the basic courses. Thus, the purpose is to analyze whether these students are more likely to dropout, delay their graduation, switch programs, or have different grade point averages (GPA). The literature has also shown that in general men are more sensitive to rankings than women (Murphy & Weinhardt, 2020; Ribas, Sampaio, & Trevisan, 2020; Dasgupta, Mani, Sharma, & Singhal, 2022) foot_33 . Nevertheless, these results differ by area, as some studies suggest that women who experience low rankings in college in STEM areas are more likely to switch programs to fields with more women (Astorne-Figari & Speer, 2019; Mouganie & Wang, 2020) .
The second objective is to analyze the existing tradeoff between the benefits of studying with high-performing peers (also referred here as competitive peers or competitive cohorts) and ranking effects, as for some students more competitive peers could do more harm (by reducing students' confidence and self-perception) than good (Hoekstra, Mouganie, & Wang, 2018; Houng & Ryan, 2018; Shi, 2020) . For this analysis, this research explores the difference in competitiveness among the cohorts that enroll in the university and the fact that the grading system that the university uses is not relative but absolute with respect to the goals of the course. The latter means that it is likely that the best performing student does not receive the highest possible grade, and in some cases (as it happens for difficult courses), a grade that is close to the passing grade.
This characteristic makes possible the identification of highly competitive cohorts, as those ones with significantly higher GPAs, and both significantly higher scores on the entrance exam and the national standardized test. This objective complements similar papers, where the competitiveness is measured in terms of the semester students enroll in the university (Ribas, Sampaio, & Trevisan, 2020) , or in terms of the competitiveness of the college within the same university (Dasgupta, Mani, Sharma, & Singhal, 2022) .
Finally, the last objective is to analyze whether providing information to students about their relative performance has effects on different educational outcomes. More specifically, whether students who were placed under "academic probation" in the first semester can improve their performance relative to students with similar performance but who were slightly above the threshold for academic probation. Because universities frequently have different policies regarding academic probation, there is no clear consensus about its effects on students' outcomes. These policies vary in the number of credits students are allowed to enroll, access to financial aid, access to supportive services, and GPA requirements to exit probation, as in some universities students must also comply with a cumulative GPA above a certain threshold (Fletcher & Tokmouline, 2010; Fletcher & Tokmouline, 2018; Yeaton & Moss, 2020) . In this study, academic probation acts more as a message that students receive stating that their performance was below the university's expectations. As such, students under academic probation receive the same treatment as any other student regarding credits, minimum GPA requirements to avoid dismissals, or access to services and financial aid.
The information comes from the University of Antioquia, a large and highly selective public university in Colombia. There are unique aspects of this dataset that allow the pursuit of the objectives described previously. First, admissions are granted based on the scores of an entrance exam that measures two components: mathematical and verbal reasoning. In this way, admissions do not consider information from students about high school performance, such as the scores on the national standardized tests taken at the end of high school. Although the admitted students usually perform above the average in their respective schools on these tests, there is significant variation in the scores reported by students. The scores on the national standardized tests (Saber 11) are used as a proxy of the performance of students in high school and provide the information to calculate high school rankings. Second, the entrance exam provides information about the exact ranking that each student obtained relative to the applicants within each program. This is a rare feature in the literature about rankings effects, because typically, students are not informed of their relative performance, and they learn only about it by interacting with their peers. The common exceptions in the literature come mainly from Randomized Control Trials (RCTs), in which some students are provided with exact information about rankings (Azmat & Iriberri, 2010; Bandiera, Larcinese, & Rasul, 2015; Tran & Zeckhauser, 2012) and others are not. Third, the curricula for the different undergraduate programs at the university are usually predetermined, and incoming students must take the same courses during the first semesters, except for usually one elective course. This implies that incoming students from each program enroll in the same courses, and it is possible to calculate their relative performance as they take the same classes. Note that this differs from other contexts such as the U.S, where students have more flexibility in the first semesters to enroll in different courses before defining a major. Fourth, by the end of each semester, students receive information about their relative GPA in the form of terciles (lower, middle, upper), whether they had an "outstanding" semester, or whether they are on academic probation.
This research also focuses on the information that rankings provide, or feedback effects, and how students react to this information to make decisions regarding study effort, educational attainment, and area of specialization. The literature on feedback effects mostly relies on experiments. In this way, students who receive feedback in the form of rankings achieve better grades in high school (Azmat & Iriberri, 2010) , college (Bandiera, Larcinese, & Rasul, 2015) , and higher scores on international English exams (Tran & Zeckhauser, 2012) . Similarly, other studies that rely on methodologies other than RCTs also find that rankings provide valuable information to students, and this information leads them to modify their behaviors. For example, Elsner, Isphording, and Zolitz (2021) using a natural experiment, where students are assigned randomly to different tutorial groups during the first year of college, find that the rankings obtained by students in each of those groups influences college performance and the selection of the specialization area, with highly ranked students taking more math-intensive courses. Goulas and Megalokonomou (2021) using data from Greece, where prior to 2005 students were required to take the national standardized test twice (each year of the last two from high school), show that those who took both tests and received a high score on the first test (positive feedback) obtained a specific exams or courses, but none of them analyze whether providing information about their relative GPAs for a semester could have impacts on students' performance.
This research also intends to provide practical tools for universities that help them to reduce dropout rates and the time to graduation. Because the University of Antioquia is highly subsidized, fees are relatively small for students and in some cases mostly symbolic. Therefore, some students take more time than expected to complete their studies. In some extreme cases, students remain enrolled for more than 20 semesters without obtaining a degree. This unfortunate outcome not only generates inefficiencies in the allocation of resources to the university, but also represents an enormous opportunity cost for students, who could be better off in other college programs or out of the university. An additional contribution is to provide insights to universities with similar conditions about what other information could be extracted from rankings that help them identify early students who are more prone to delay their graduation, drop out, or experience difficulties in their studies.
Rankings are calculated using the scores on the national standardized tests that students take at the end of the final year of high school (Saber 11) and the GPA obtained during the first semester of college. These rankings are measured with respect to each student's cohort. It is important to highlight that rankings are not perfectly correlated to any measure of absolute performance, and a specific score on the standardized test (or GPA) does not guarantee any student a determined position in the rankings. For example, if a student who got a score "x" on the national standardized test attends high school A, which has limited resources and less competitive peers, and this score grants her the best position in her school, she would not necessarily keep the same position had she attended school B, a more selective school. The intended empirical strategy to address objectives 1 and 2 is the use of "value-added" education production functions. These types of functions are frequently used to estimate GPA and standardized test scores as functions of previous achievements and contemporaneous socioeconomic characteristics. Their main assumption is that including achievements from previous stages eliminates the potential bias produced by unobservable characteristics, such as innate ability (Todd & Wolpin, 2003) . Attending college and selecting a college program are decisions influenced not only by socioeconomic characteristics, but by students' performance in previous educational levels such as secondary school, which makes these models suitable for estimations. Further, controlling for previous achievement washes out other characteristics that could confound the effect of rankings. It is important to highlight that the definition of competitiveness for this study is based on the three variables mentioned previously. This definition intends to capture not only the general knowledge, innate ability, and the skills of students at taking exams, as measured by the scores on Saber 11 and the entrance exam, but also other traits that determine the success of students in college, such as perseverance, motivation, discipline, and effort, which can be captured by the GPA in the first semester. Overall, higher rankings in high school are correlated with more desirable outcomes in college, independently of students' performance in the first semester of college. While some of the estimations are not significantly different from zero, there seem to be specific trends depending on the segment of the distribution of college performance. In this sense, higher high school rankings are correlated to higher GPAs in college, but the effect is only significant at the top of the distribution of college performance in the first semester (group high) and after two and four years of enrollment. The maximum average effect ranges between 0.25 to 0.30 point increases in GPA, which is equivalent to multiplying the estimated coefficient by the range of the rankings (1, as the lowest and highest rankings are 0 and 1 respectively). This means that there are only significant and positive effects on GPA for those students who were at the top of their cohorts in high school and continued being at the top in college (figure 3.2a). Similarly, higher rankings in high school are correlated with students enrolling in (and earning) more credits, but these effects are only significant for the second semester of college and for those students who had the highest GPAs in the first semester (figures 3.2b and 3.2c). There are two potential explanations for this outcome:
students who obtain GPAs higher than 4.0 and earn all credits are allowed to enroll for more credits, and students who obtained the relatively highest GPAs in the first semester are less likely to withdraw credits, as withdrawn credits are not counted as enrolled credits.
At the bottom of the performance in the first semester of college (low group), students with higher high school rankings are less likely to be expelled and on average less likely to fail credits 2 and 4 years after they enrolled in college (figures 3.2d and 3.2e), although the latter effect is not significantly different from zero. The former result could be read as students who were at the top of their cohorts in high school (and have a ranking of 1) are 16% less likely to be expelled than students who were at the bottom (ranking equals 0). Interestingly, students with higher rankings are more likely to switch programs, and this effect is more significant for the high and low groups.
Two conclusions can be inferred from this section. First, changes in rankings do not seem to have any significant correlation with different college outcomes. Students who came from being at the top of their classes in high school to the bottom in the first semester of college are not particularly discouraged, which could lead them to lower college outcomes. In fact, the results seem to point in the opposite direction: they are less likely to be expelled relative to students with lower high school rankings. Similarly, albeit not significant, students with higher high school rankings and relatively low performance in the first semester of college have on average higher GPAs and credits earned in the following semesters. Second, these results are in line with the long-lasting effects of students' rankings in secondary education on college outcomes (and even post-college outcomes) analyzed in other settings (Elsner & Isphording, 2017; Murphy & Weinhardt, 2020) .

This study addresses three questions related to ranking effects. First, whether students who were at the top of their classes in high school react in the same way as average (or bottom-ranked) students when both experience low (or high) performance in the first semester if college; second, whether enrolling in the most and least competitive cohorts by program translates into differential outcomes for students; and third, whether students who receive an academic probation in the first semester, which acts only as a message to improve their performance in college, obtain better outcomes in the following semesters. For these three objectives, this study analyzes various outcomes in college measured at different times.

The estimations show that students with higher high school rankings obtain on average better outcomes in college. This result could be interpreted as evidence that changes in rankings are not relevant for students. It also points to the long-lasting effects of high school rankings to explain academic results in college and the importance of considering such rankings to help detect the students who are more prone to be expelled or switch programs. Thus, it is not surprising that universities around the world use high school rankings for admission purposes. Students enrolled in more competitive cohorts, as measured by the average scores on the college entrance exam, the national standardized tests, and the median GPA in the first semester, exhibit worse college outcomes than any other cohort, particularly in terms of GPA, and credits earned and failed. On the other hand, the educational outcomes for students enrolled in noncompetitive cohorts are usually not different from any other cohort. Although it is not possible to disentangle whether this effect is explained by the tradeoff between better peers and ranking effects, or simply by professors being more demanding when students seem more knowledgeable, identifying which cohorts are more (or less) competitive could provide valuable information to colleges. Additionally, the fact that more competitive cohorts have negative effects only on the top students, such as in the case of GPA, and not on the other groups could suggest that the tradeoff between high-performing peers and ranking effects varies by group. In this way, students who struggle in college could benefit from high performing peers in their classes, but high performing peers do not see benefits, at least in terms of grades or credits, from studying in more competitive cohorts. Nonetheless, further research is necessary to draw definitive conclusions, especially when the definition of competitiveness affects the results.

This program was introduced in the 1990s, and it is the most important program in the country to incentivize agricultural family production. The program is open to any type of production, not only soybeans. It considers three types of funding: production, infrastructure and municipal services, and training (Junqueira & de Lima, 2008) . Brazilian policymakers recognize family farmers as households whose land sizes vary from 5 to 110 hectares, depending on the locality, use family labor as the main input, and their income comes predominantly from agricultural activities. All funded projects by Pronaf must comply with environmental regulations. In case of infringements, farmers might lose access to credit in the future. In line with the environmental regulations, the program excludes households living in environmentally risky areas, such as in the Caatinga biome (Zeller & Schiesari, 2020) . Pronaf also targets farmers with different socioeconomic levels. In this way, it is designed to include family farmers classified in Group A (farmers settled by the land reform), Group B (farmers who live below the poverty line), and Group V (farmers with high income levels). Additional requirements include: i) obtaining at least 50% of the family's gross income from the farm, ii) a gross family income of no more than to R$500,000 (Reais) in the last 12 (around US$93,000), iii) permanent employees must be no greater than the number of family members employed in the farm.

Different from SCM, Panel data with Fixed Effects estimations do not meet the necessary conditions to interpret the results as causal. Thus, the control group is not equivalent to the treatment group in the absence of the treatment. This means that even though the estimations using Panel data include causal effects, these effects are contaminated by differences in unobserved characteristics and differences in trends.
