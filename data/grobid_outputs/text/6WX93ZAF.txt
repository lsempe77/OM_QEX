Graduation model programs expand on the concept of targeted cash or food transfers supporting consumption by providing an additional lump-sum asset or cash transfer designed to promote and sustain poverty alleviation. These graduation programs often include intensive livelihood training to support higher earnings as well as measures to promote financial inclusion (Banerjee et al. 2015; Bandiera et al. 2017; Bossuroy et al. 2022). Occasionally, they also include explicit measures to increase women's empowerment or reduce stress. Graduation models have been tested in many countries after the publication of results of trials in six countries styled after BRAC's Targeting the Extremely Poor program (Banerjee et al. 2015). Evaluations of these programs have demonstrated their potential to improve economic outcomes. Most of these programs, however, were not designed with nutrition interventions and generally do not report effects on nutrition outcomes.
Graduation model programs may be strengthened by including nutrition objectives and explicit nutrition programming for several reasons. Malnutrition is often, but not always, concentrated in poor households that are targeted in graduation programs. Thus, reduction of malnutrition is consistent with the objective of achieving sustainable long-term improvements in well-being for participants and their communities and to address outcomes beyond monetary poverty. There is some evidence that transfers must be accompanied by other design features such as nutrition training and access to health services to be effective in achieving nutrition-related objectives (Manley, Alderman, and Gentilini 2022; Ahmed, Hoddinott, and Roy 2024). Still, currently little is known about how to design graduation programs in terms of the size of the asset transfer and the approach to nutrition services to successfully achieve improvements in child nutrition.
The current study aims to address this lacuna. It reports the results of a cluster randomized controlled trial to assess the effect on child nutrition of a "lighttouch" graduation program, Strengthen PSNP Institutions and Resilience (SPIR). The program included nutrition interventions of varying intensity provided to beneficiaries of Ethiopia's Productive Safety Net Program (PSNP), one of the largest social protection programs on the continent. 1 Implemented at scale, SPIR also provided PSNP beneficiaries with additional livelihood programming including savings-group formation, agricultural and livestock value-chain training, and linkages to financial services. In addition, consistent with the objective of enhancing livelihoods, extremely poor households received a one-time additional transfer, either as cash transfers or in-kind poultry grants. Leight et al. (2023) compare the size of the lump-sum transfer in the current trial with 10 others reported in the literature; all but Bossuroy et al. (2022) exceeded the value of the current program in 2017 purchasing power parity dollars. The difference is often substantial. For example, transfers in the six programs studied in Banerjee et al. (2015) were up to four times larger and were accompanied by intensive livelihoods training and weekly household-level technical support and coaching.
Child stunting and infant and young child feeding (IYCF) practices and knowledge were the primary and secondary nutrition outcomes evaluated by this trial, which was adequately powered to assess a significant difference in these outcomes. This paper makes two main contributions to the literature on graduation. First, it documents the contribution of modestly sized cash and in-kind livelihood support on nutrition. Second, it indicates whether the effect of such grants can be enhanced with the provision of nutritional services of varying intensity. Results show that contact with health-care workers and participation in community health activities increased significantly under the enhanced nutrition model, as did maternal nutritional knowledge. Nevertheless, in the absence of an additional grant, the enhanced nutrition model did not achieve significant improvements in child stunting nor did the core nutrition model with grants have an observable effect on this primary outcome. However, some complementarity of program components was observed. In particular, poultry livelihood grants combined with the enhanced nutrition model increased child dietary diversity, and cash livelihood grants combined with the enhanced nutrition model resulted in significantly reduced childhood stunting.
Behavioral change communication (BCC) is a regular component of nutrition interventions. Keats et al. (2021) deem the evidence on the contribution of BCC to effective implementation of breast-feeding promotion and to education on complementary feeding in conjunction with food provision to be strong in foodinsecure settings but only moderate without food provision in more food-secure populations. This implies potential program complementarity with transfer programs (Olney et al. 2022). A few programs that include regular grants during the critical years for child growth as well as packages of services along with nutrition-focused BCC have reported significant effects on child nutrition (Cahyadi et al. 2020; Carneiro et al. 2021). Relatively few studies, however, have directly assessed the effect of BCC in conjunction with transfers compared with transfers alone (Field and Maffioli 2021; Little et al. 2021; Ahmed, Hoddinott, and Roy 2024). Although the ultimate goal of behavioral change is to improve nutritional outcomes, studies often focus on knowledge and behaviors around IYCF, which appear more responsive to interventions than do anthropometric indicators. For example, Han, Kim, and Park (2021) look at BCC combined with food vouchers in Ethiopia and conclude that neither intervention alone influences child feeding practices or chronic child undernutrition, but the combination does.
This is the first study we are aware of to provide systematic evidence on the effectiveness of nutrition BCC delivered at scale as an integral part of a graduation program on child growth for extremely poor households. While the literature on graduation programs regularly examines effects on food security, relatively few studies look at child nutritional status or dietary diversity among transfer recipients. For example, none of the six country studies in the seminal paper by Banerjee et al. (2015) nor those by Bandiera et al. (2017), Blattman, Fiala, and Martinez (2020), or Bossuroy et al. (2022) include evidence on child stunting or underweight. 2 Haushofer and Shapiro (2016) report results on an overall health index in which nutrition is a component but find no effect from either the monthly or the larger lump-sum transfer on this composite measure. McIntosh and Zeitlin (2024) show the effect of lump-sum transfers on nutritional outcomes and compare the effect with that of an in-kind nutritional and maternal health intervention with cash transfers in Rwanda and find significant effects on anthropometric outcomes in one sub-arm of the cash component in the study. They do not, however, explore the complementarity of cash in health interventions.
As well as comparing cash transfers with in-kind services, McIntosh and Zeitlin (2024) include a range of transfer allocations to benchmark the size of transfers that might result in improved nutrition. They find that only the largest transfer, which was more than twice as large as the one in the light-touch graduation program studied here, had an effect of 0.1 standard deviations on anthropometry, significant at the 10% level. 3 The current study builds on those findings by investigating whether a considerably smaller transfer than provided in several published graduation programs can enhance child nutritional status by exploiting complementarities in scalable nutrition-focused services.
In addition to exploring complementarity within a graduation program, the study's experimental design compares the effect of two models of nutrition investment, an enhanced model of more intensive BCC along with components designed to strengthen male support to investments in child nutrition and support to women's well-being, including mental health, and a less intensive community nutrition program that did not directly support household visits. The goal of the intensive mode of delivery is in keeping with Kim et al. (2020), who report results of trials in Bangladesh, Ethiopia, and Vietnam designed to directly compare intensive interpersonal contact relative to alternate standard nutrition BCC programs. All three of these studies confirmed that the frequency of contact matters for dietary diversity and breast-feeding practices. Kim et al. (2019) also modeled dose response for improved height for age with a combination of platforms in a companion study in Ethiopia. The current study-investigating a program that reached more than one-half million beneficiaries-adds to the literature on nutrition program intensity within the context of an integrated graduation model program.
While the PSNP was initiated in 2005, it did not include a specific nutrition component until the fourth phase (PSNP4) launched in 2015 (Berhane et al. 2020). To date, neither the earlier stages nor the fourth phase of PSNP have shown any acceleration of the national trend in reducing stunting of young children (Berhane et al. 2020; Abay et al. 2023). 4 The US Agency for International Development (USAID) Bureau of Humanitarian Assistance supported the PSNP4 in selected woredas (districts) through the SPIR graduation program. In addition to supporting implementation of PSNP4, SPIR also provided additional complementary livelihood, nutrition, gender, and natural resource management activities led by World Vision in partnership with the Organization for Rehabilitation and Development in Amhara and CARE. SPIR targeted more than 500,000 PNSP clients in 15 of the most vulnerable woredas in Amhara and Oromia regions of Ethiopia and provided community-level programming and capacity building of government staff involved in public-service delivery at the woreda (district) and kebele (subdistrict) level.
The trial included three treatment arms and a control group. One treatment arm provided a package of enhanced nutrition (EN) services including support for household-level BCC, recuperative feeding for acutely malnourished children, promotion of male engagement in household tasks, and group therapy for mothers screened for depressive symptoms. A subset targeting extremely poor beneficiaries within this arm also received an additional one-time grant either in cash or as a poultry production start-up package as a part of the enhanced livelihood program component. A second treatment arm provided the enhanced livelihood program treatment including the provision of grants to extremely poor households with a less intensive and less costly core nutrition (CN) service. 5 A third treatment arm provided the same enhanced package of nutrition services as the first treatment arm but without the enhanced livelihood program. All three treatment arms also provided a set of core services including promotion of savings and access to credit through Village Economic and Social Associations (VESAs).
Extremely poor households among the already food-insecure PSNP client households in the two enhanced livelihood program treatment arms received a livelihood transfer equivalent to US$200 in local currency. These grants are not a general component of the PSNP program but mirror the livelihoods transfer provision in the PSNP4 program implementation manual. The kebeles in these arms of the study were randomly selected to provide either a one-time cash transfer or a poultry package of 16 birds as well as feed, equipment, and cash for veterinary services. The poultry package, including services, is equivalent in value to the cash grants. In each of these kebeles, the 10 poorest households out of 18 households in the baseline survey sample, the "extremely poor," were chosen to receive these transfers through a ranking assessment based on an asset index. The asset index included ownership data on more than 30 asset categories, including consumer durables, productive assets, livestock, and land. It was constructed by using principal components analysis, in which the first component maximizes the variation of all variables explained by that component, and therefore gives high weights to variables that are highly correlated with each other (Filmer and Pritchett 2001). The asset indexes and the rankings were constructed for all arms of the study, including the control arm, in order to have comparable samples. These extremely poor households are the main focus of this study. 6 The Consolidated Standards of Reporting Trials (CONSORT) diagram (fig. 1 ) summarizes the research design and stratification and indicates sample sizes. As indicated, while the overall trial had three treatment arms, because there were two modalities of livelihood grants, these three arms can be further divided into five subarms for analysis.
Across all treatment arms, as well as in the control group, PSNP clients participating in public works were eligible to attend six monthly 2-hour nutrition BCC sessions in place of 4 person-days of work over the 6-month period. This was augmented by IYCF and nutrition BCC discussions in VESAs in both the CN and EN treatments. Topics covered included optimal IYCF practices, adolescent and maternal nutrition, diversified sources of nutritious foods, and utilization of health and nutrition services. Also in all treatment arms, a water, sanitation, and hygiene (WASH) component included implementation of a community-led total sanitation and hygiene activity and open defecation free (ODF) public-education events, as well as the provision of support to village-level WASH management activities and limited support to improving potable water and sanitation infrastructure (water sources and latrines).
The EN interventions built upon these core nutrition activities by supporting government health extension staff and volunteers to additionally deliver BCC lessons on IYCF practices and adolescent and maternal nutrition through a timed and targeted counseling (TTC) model at the household level. The TTC model envisions 11 home visits in the first 1,000 days after conception, 4 of which should be during pregnancy, 2 in the first month postpartum, 4 more when the child is between 1 and 12 months, and another visit when the child is between 14 and 18 months. Additional services in the EN treatment kebeles were targeted to caregivers of children screened for moderate or severe malnutritionbased underweight (weight for age). Children with moderate acute malnutrition based on mid-upper arm circumference (MUAC) were also invited to participate, although more severe cases were referred to health centers. These communitybased participatory nutrition promotion (CPNP) sessions included training on complementary feeding and caring practices within 2-week intensive recuperative feeding sessions.
These activities were coordinated by trained community health facilitators who were recruited and assigned to each of the kebeles to support the health extension workers (HEWs) in planning, coordinating, and facilitating health and nutrition activities. The facilitators also supported local Health Development Army (HDA) volunteers to conduct household-level counseling related to IYCF and maternal nutrition by using the TTC approach. Both husband and wife were encouraged to be present for these counseling sessions to promote male engagement in targeted IYCF and maternal nutrition actions. In addition, local facilitators supported the formation of men's engagement groups and facilitated eight sessions designed to critically reflect on cultural gender norms and explore the positive and perceived negative effects of male involvement in household chores and child-care activities that were typically assigned as women's-only tasks. 7 Figure 2 summarizes these different nutrition-and livelihood-related services in each arm including those available for the PSNP participants in the control. CN activities are additional to those available for the control, and the services in EN build upon those for the CN arm.
The data were collected over three rounds, with a baseline conducted between February and April 2018, a midline survey between July and October 2019, and an end-line survey originally planned for 2020, 24 months after the baseline, but delayed an additional 12 months because of COVID-19. Initially, 196 kebeles in Amhara (115 kebeles) and Oromia (81 kebeles) regions were selected for the trial and subsequently randomized into treatment groups. However, two kebeles had no PSNP clients and thus were not eligible for the program. Two other kebeles experienced ongoing civil unrest and were necessarily dropped from the project. Thus, the evaluation sample comprises 192 kebeles. In each kebele, up to 18 households were randomly sampled, leading to a planned baseline sample of 3,456 households in the study. The inclusion criteria for the sample were that households had to (1) be a PSNP client household, (2) have at least one child aged 0-35 months (the index child), and (3) have the mother or primary female caregiver of the 0-to-35-month-old child as a member of the household. 8 The last criterion enabled measurement of maternal and child diets as well as child care for nutritionally vulnerable ages in all sample households. The eventual baseline sample consisted of 3,314 households.
The end-line survey sought to reinterview any baseline household unless it had permanently moved or dissolved. Of the 3,248 households in the end-line target sample, 3,094 were located and interviewed, implying an attrition rate of 4.7% relative to the target sample, or 6.6% relative to the original baseline sample. Nearly half of the attrition (70 households, or 2% of the target sample) at end line was due to insecurity in one of the operational woredas in Amhara that resulted in a decision not to visit four kebeles. Among the remaining 84 attrited households, 48 had moved out of the study area, 24 were temporarily unavailable, and other households had either dissolved or were unavailable for the interview for other reasons; one household refused consent. In total, 3,062 primary female respondents and 2,482 primary male respondents were surveyed.
Regressions with the dependent variable defined as 1 if an extremely poor household in the baseline was not available for the end line indicate that household attrition was not associated with the treatment status relative to the control (see app. A), although there was a small difference between two treatment arms. The results in columns 1 and 2 of table A1 exclude the four kebeles that were lost because of insecurity under the assumption that the insecurity was exogenous. As a check on whether attrition influences the outcomes studied, we perform a test on the potential effect of both observable and unobservable attributes of attrition on outcomes by regressing height for age in the baseline sample against a set of covariates including a dummy variable defined as 1 if the household is subsequently lost to the sample, including those households in communities dropped because of insecurity (Fitzgerald, Gottschalk, and Moffitt 1998). The results, in column 3 of table A2 , indicate that there is no association between subsequent attrition and child stature.
The delay in the end-line data collection necessitated by COVID-19 meant that the initial sample children were older than 24 months by the end line. However, 905 of the baseline index children had younger siblings less than 24 months who were included in the end-line sample and contributed to outcome measures for children less than 2 years old at end line. Even so, the sample for the analysis of current feeding practices and contact with health-care workers is smaller than at the baseline. Columns 3 and 4 of table A1 test the probability that a household from the baseline has a child in the end-line sample-a function both of the commonly used definition of household attrition and of its fertility choices. While the treatment arm that provided the EN services but did not offer grants has no significant attrition at the household level, there is a significant decrease in the probability that a household in this arm has a child in the end-line sample. However, there is no association of the risk of attrition and the primary height for age outcome (table A2 , col. 2).
The end-line household interview was conducted in three parts: householdlevel questions covering household and respondent identification and household demographics; a set of questions for the identified primary male respondent; and a set of questions for the identified primary female respondent. The primary female was the mother of the index child in most cases, and the primary male was her partner. A separate, specifically trained team conducted anthropometry measurements.

In order to shed light on the program components that are designed to influence the primary outcome of nutrition, we also investigated the impact of the EN and CN models on access to the services provided and on any changes in diets. Some of these secondary results are explored in child-level regressions and some are based on household-or mother-level results. In the latter category, we modify equation ( 1 ) to employ analysis of covariance models that include the household baseline outcome as a right-hand-side regressor. 9 The child-specific models are restricted to different age groups depending on features of the outcome studied. Thus, the anthropometry regression, which covers children up to 36 months, includes a variable for age in months. In contrast, the diet data cover a smaller sample of children up to 24 months because the module was not administered either to parents of children above 24 months or to parents of children less than 6 months for whom exclusive breast-feeding is strongly recommended. In the case of anthropometry, age is included as a regressor to accommodate differences in growth velocity and biological needs of children as well as the cumulative effect of programs (Alderman and Headey 2018). In addition to child's age and gender, which are included as controls in child-specific models, all models control for whether the mother has any education, as well as for whether the day before the interview was a fasting day in case of dietary outcomes.
While we present individual outcomes, where appropriate we also report average standard treatment effects (ASTEs) estimated for key families of outcomes, following Kling, Liebman, and Katz (2007). These are aggregated summaries of key outcomes of interest equivalent to outcomes presented in the tables where all variables could be added up into a single measure. The ASTEs serve as summary indexes that aggregate information over multiple outcomes and present effect sizes relative to the standard deviation of the control arm. This aggregation improves statistical power to detect effects that go in the same direction within a domain. As this calculation requires that the signs of the coefficients all have the same welfare interpretation, it is occasionally necessary to redefine the outcome for the purpose of computing an ASTE. We report ASTEs for both nutrition programs as well as the two transfers, poultry or cash.
Where the dependent variable is a count item-either knowledge scores or the number of food categories consumed-the total score serves in lieu of the ASTE.
Table 1 shows that the sample is well balanced across treatment arms in baseline characteristics. There are a few differences across treatment arms significant at p < :10, but these do not appear to be systematic in any direction. Moreover, there are no significant differences between treatment arms and the control in the key anthropometry measures. These summary statistics provide an overview of the context at baseline. The low average of expenditures is consistent with the fact that the households in the sample are the poorest among a population eligible for the PSNP. The kebeles are fairly remote at 11-14 km from the nearest town. Households have 5.3-5.6 members reflecting the fact that the children in the sample are younger siblings of children in the baseline who are no longer in the sample but remain in the household. Education levels are low, with only 1 in 5 women and 1 in 3 men having any education. Both women's and children's diets are limited, with only 3.4% of women meeting the standard for minimum dietary diversity (FAO and USAID 2015) and index children aged 6-35 months consuming only three food groups per day. Virtually none of these index children consumed a minimally acceptable diet (WHO 2017). Child malnutrition is a substantial public-health problem in these communities: across the different treatment arms, 38%-42% of index children were stunted.
Figure 3 illustrates the trend in stunting over the survey rounds. In all communities, stunting rates increased, likely reflecting the COVID shock as well as localized rainfall shortages (Hirvonen et al. 2023) in addition to significant crop losses due to pest infestations (desert locusts and fall armyworm). While there were no significant differences in stunting level across treatment arms at baseline, there is a visual indication that grants combined with nutrition services provided some protection from these shocks. The results reported in table 2 explore this pattern within a regression framework. While there was no observed difference in anthropometry relative to the control among these extremely poor households in the kebeles that received EN but did not receive the livelihood grants, those that received cash transfers had significantly higher height for age and had reduced stunting. This combination of cash and EN reduced stunting by 18 percentage points, compared with the 54% of the extremely poor in the control group that were stunted. In contrast, cash transfers provided to households receiving CN interventions did not lead to improvement in these outcomes. While the interaction of poultry and EN is not significantly different from zero, it is also not significantly different from the complementary cash and EN results and both grants are jointly significant, reducing stunting by 12.5 percentage points when combined with EN. The effect on stunting rates of the cash and EN joint intervention is similar for children 6-23 months and for those 24-25 months (app. C). However, the change in HAZ is much larger for the older children.
Next, we examine the extent to which this pattern of effects reflects deviation from the planned implementation of the program components in some treatment arms. The ASTEs reported in table 3 indicate increased access to nutrition services for the extremely poor households. This improved access is across all the components of the ASTE for the kebeles receiving the EN program, while the ASTE results for the households in the CN arm are largely driven by increased participation in community-level nutrition discussions within the VESAs. Unlike the results for stunting, the increase in service access observed in the EN program does not differ between the households receiving livelihood grants and those in the EN arms that were not provided grants. That is, there is no evidence that cash transfers drove increased health-service utilization.

The EN program appears relatively effective compared with the CN intervention due to the latter performing less well than the control. Table 5 indicates that there was an insignificant increase in maternal knowledge in the EN treatment areas. The point estimate of the average total increase in the knowledge score in EN kebeles offering grants of 0.154 reported in column 1 measures the sum of the seven questions on nutritional knowledge. 10 This effect is less than 4% of the mean score for the control population despite the EN arms receiving grants and having increased contact with HEW and HDA workers as well as participation in facilitated discussions on nutrition in the VESA meetings. This compares with the similarly modest effect of 0.226 (6.6%) on the increase on EN based on a mother having attended any school. 11 The overall EN program also contributed to an increase in the maternal dietary diversity score (col. 1 of table 6), which is based on the sum of results in columns 3-12. The increase of 0.046 in minimum dietary diversity (MDD) among women receiving poultry is small, yet it is an 81% increase over the value in the control communities, reflecting the very poor quality of diets among all mothers in the study. This increase is not significantly different than the increase among extremely poor households in the EN sample that did not receive grants. Similarly, there is no significant difference in dietary diversity if the EN community was randomized to receive grants or only the EN intervention. When all three sub-arms of the EN intervention are pooled, the average increase in dietary diversity was 0.188. This is significant at p < :05. This leaves a puzzle as to the actual mechanism of the complementarity of cash and nutritional services. The cash grants were equivalent to 11% of average annual household expenditures of recipients in the year of receipt. As lump-sum windfalls they contributed to a modest increase of both real and financial assets but did not translate to a measurable increase in total expenditures (Leight et al. 2023). While the study was able to document that the intervention served to moderate drought effects including those on household diets (Hirvonen et al. 2023), the research was not designed to track consumption smoothing over the time period between the allocation of grants in 2018 and the end-line survey in 2021. This may be part of the puzzle of how the cash grants contributed to protecting nutritional status. Additional studies of how lump-sum transfers are used for consumption smoothing in the face of income fluctuations can add to the literature on savings and asset formation with livelihood programs. Plausibly the recipients of cash were able to purchase more food in each year and to allocate more of the household food resources to children. As the dietary diversity index does not measure changes in quantities but only the probability that a food group was included in the daily diet, this mechanism is not directly measurable with the available data. In addition to possible smoothing of food expenditure over time, cash recipients were more likely to participate in the CPNP recuperative child feeding sessions, perhaps drawing upon the cash to provide the expected food inputs in this 2-week program, an issue that might be investigated further when designing similar programs with in-kind contributions by participants expected. Thus, the overall results provide a basis for tempered nutritional outcome expectations for this additional support to extremely poor households, valued at well over 1 year of transfers in the PSNP, when combined with enhanced nutrition programming.
The grant of poultry did enhance the EN program with regard to the inclusion of eggs in a child's diet, but this was not sufficient to have a significant effect on height for age. The challenge to the advocacy of household poultry production as a means to foster better child nutrition may hinge on the ability to sustain flocks. The susceptibility of the birds to illness was noted by Passarelli et al. (2022) in a similar program in Ethiopia. However, the final SPIR survey did not report on the number of birds that died. The survey did ascertain the number of birds sold in the previous 12 months; poultry grant recipients sold, on average, two more birds than their neighbors. Because of the delay in data collection necessitated by COVID, the time between surveys was greater than the 12 months recall, so there is a possibility that sales in this entire period partially account for the trend toward convergence of flock size between in-kind grant recipients and their counterparts. Nevertheless, the potential to enhance consumption of animal-source foods with these transfers did not prove to be sustainable (app. D).
The clearest evidence of the effect of the EN treatment distinct from the complementarity with grants was shown in the increase of contact between mothers of young children and service providers despite the challenges of COVID as well as the improvement in women's IYCF knowledge. Within the EN kebeles, the overall probability of any mother meeting with an HEWor member of the HDA in the previous 3 months increased from both the baseline as well as the pre-COVID midline. Still, even though the contact in EN communities was 28% above the 43% rate of contact for mothers in the control, it was not sufficient to improve anthropometric indicators of child nutrition status. This likely reflects the fact that the frequency of contact with health-service providers (either HEWs or HDA volunteers) still fell well short of intent. Although the TTC model envisions 11 home visits in the first 1,000 days after conception, the end-line survey revealed that approximately only a quarter of mothers of children under 12 months in the EN communities reported a home visit in the past 3 months, and this probability declined for older children.
This contrasts with an average of two visits in the past 3 months in Kim et al. (2020), which likely accounts for the larger effect measured in that study. However, there is a positive association of HAZ and program participation for children whose household reported having received a home visit recently. This hints at the possibility that more frequent service provision might have more favorable effects, but it does not give any indication as to how this intensification may be achieved. Because the current study cannot assess the determinants of home visits by HEWs or HDA volunteers, this can be considered a limitation of the study and an area for future research.
Restricting the sample of all children-both extremely poor and less poor (app. C) to the 598 young children in the households that reported they had contact with an HEW or HDA volunteer in the preceding 3 months (a selected subsample that is potentially biased toward larger effects)-finds an increase in HAZ of 0.286 (SE 5 0:163) and a reduction of stunting of 20.082 (SE 5 0:045). Both these increases are significant at the 10% level, reflecting, in part, the lower statistical power due to sample restriction. A further restriction of the sample to the 253 households where the HEW or HDA volunteer visited at the household indicated much larger point estimates of improvements in HAZ and reduction in stunting, 0.616 (SE 5 0:275) and 20.165 (SE 5 0:072), respectively, with both of these estimates significant at the 5% level. While the unknown nature of the selectivity into this subsample, possibly due to bettertrained staff and supervisors or more motivated mothers or both, makes these results only suggestive at best, they may provide guidance for future inclusion of nutrition within the PSNP and is a promising subject for future research.
Drilling down further, because the timing of TTC implies that contact with service providers should be greatest when a child is under a year old, although some contact is expected after the first birthday as well, we test this by interacting a dummy variable for older children in the regressions that assess access to health services. For example, in an alternative specification of the results in column 3 of table 3 (not shown), the coefficient for all EN programs is 0.166 (SE 5 0:043). However, there does not seem to be any visits at home when children are 12-24 months; the coefficient for that age group is 0.022 (SE 5 0:037). This is significantly different from the younger age group (p < :01).
While the less intensive CN intervention led to increased participation in community events relative to the control, it did not lead to increased contact with government health staff or volunteers or to increased women's IYCF knowledge or dietary diversity. Even though women in the CN arm reported the inclusion of nutrition topics in VESAs similar to those in the EN arm, all the increases in contact with health-service providers in the EN program were larger than those in the CN program ( p < :10). This difference likely explains the absence of an effect on mother's knowledge score or her dietary diversity in the CN program, outcomes that were influenced in the EN arm of the study.
Despite the increased nutritional knowledge among women as well as changes in their diets, there was not a clear effect on dietary diversity for young children in the EN program. This remains extremely low. Hirvonen et al. (2021) document late introduction of solid and semisolid foods in Ethiopia that is linked to lower length for age of young children. In the current study, 26% of the children aged 6-8 months consumed no other food than breastmilk in the previous day indicated in the list of 25 foods in the appropriate IYCF module for dietary diversity.
On the basis of the line-item budget records of the three nongovernmental organizations providing services for the nutrition program components, the cost per household to deliver the CN was US$18.8. The EN program included an additional US$32.5 in budgeted services. 13 These are marginal financial expenditures over the full 5 years of program implementation, excluding services provided as part of the PSNP received by all households. As is often the case with large-scale BCC programs, the HDA volunteers were unpaid. Although the opportunity cost of these volunteers' time does not enter into the service providers' budgets, these are economic costs that should be considered. The time commitment of these volunteers per household over the project timeline was US$1.84. Similarly, because households need to attend various meetings and community events in order to benefit from the nutrition interventions, there is also an economic cost incurred by these households. We estimate the former opportunity cost for travel by EN-arm households over the 3 years until the child's third birthday to be US$0.48 for BCC not in the household and US$0.81 for attending community sanitation events. The costs of both the cash and the poultry grants are US$200 per household excluding the targeting costs for the randomization, which were based on the baseline survey data.
In conclusion, the success in enhancing coverage of nutrition services to lowincome mothers in remote areas can be considered a partial achievement of the SPIR program. It remains, however, a challenge to maximize the nutritional effect of this service delivery. Livelihood grants make a modest contribution to the challenge, but in the longer run, it is a larger task to build upon this contribution.

Appendix D Testing the Persistence of Results of Poultry Ownership Table D1 compares the midline results from Alderman et al. ( 2022 ) with the end-line effect a year and a half later. The specification replicates the earlier results by using the full sample and including a supplementary sample added at midline to refresh the sample of children less than 24 months of age. Thus, the specification differs slightly from the results presented in the main text. The opportunity to return to the initial results indicates that the significant increase in egg consumption for all households eligible for poultry grants that was observed at midline is no longer apparent. This is likely driven by the fact that at midline-less than 6 months after the grants were distributed-poultry recipients had 7.5 more birds than households that did not receive the transfer, including those who had a cash grant of equivalent value. Two years after the grants were disbursed, this difference had declined to only 1.5 birds, although the increase in poultry owned remained statistically significant (table D1 , col. 8). However, the coefficient of the interaction of enhanced nutrition and poultry in column 6 reproduces the result in table 7 despite the slight differences in sample and specification.

The cost estimates are based on the sum of the appropriate line items in the budgets of World Vision, CARE, and the Organization for Rehabilitation and Development in Amhara. These costs are across 5 years and cover the program costs for all households receiving the programs, including the roughly 500,000 not in the sample. In general, it is appropriate to divide by the total number of program participants to estimate unit household costs for many of the SPIR services. Because of age targeting, however, a share of the households did not participate in the nutrition programs continually over the entire period studied. Thus, to get the cost per nutrition program potential beneficiary household, we required a means to allocate the marginal nutrition program costs over households that had a child between the ages of 0 and 36 months at any time during the 5-year period of implementation. Given that all households in the sample survey used in this study had a young child at baseline, this dataset is not a suitable source for calculating the unit costs. Therefore, we used the share of PSNP beneficiaries in a sample collected in 2016 (Headey et al. 2019) to estimate that 78% of household in Amhara and Oromia had a child in the age range 0-8 years, that is, the share of households that had at least one child that was between 0 and 3 years in the preceding 5 years. The opportunity cost of time for the HDA volunteers is based on their time allocation per household reported in the end-line survey multiplied by the average public-works wage in Amhara and Oromia. We applied this time allocation to cover 5 years to be comparable with the budget data. Similarly, we use the public-works wage to estimate the opportunity cost of beneficiaries for participation in BCC programs and community hygiene and sanitation events. These data come from recall reported for the previous 3 months in the end-line survey. We approximate the cost of the child's vulnerable years by assuming that the time reported is constant and thus multiply the 3-month time commitment by 12 to cover 3 years. 14 Participants also received training on nutrition in VESAs.
